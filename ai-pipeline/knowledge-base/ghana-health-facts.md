# Ghana Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

## Condition: Malaria

### Profile
Malaria remains a hyper-endemic health challenge across all 16 regions of Ghana, accounting for a significant percentage of outpatient clinic visits. It is caused by the *Plasmodium falciparum* parasite and transmitted via the bites of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Shivering chills (*asonson*) followed by elevated body temperature (high fever).
* Intense, persistent headache, joint weakness, and severe bodily fatigue.
* Bitter taste in the mouth, nausea, loss of appetite, and vomiting.
* In infants: unexplained lethargy, hotness of the body, and refusal to breastfeed.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Malaria, Plasmodium falciparum, Anopheles mosquito.
* **Akan (Twi / Fante):** Hurae, asonson, nwunu (chills).
* **Ga:** Atridii.
* **Ewe:** Asrã.

### National Management Protocols
* **Diagnostic Mandate:** The Ghana Health Service strictly enforces the "T3" strategy: **Test, Treat, and Track**. Every suspected malaria case must be confirmed using a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or microscopy before administering drugs.
* **First-Line Intervention:** Confirmed uncomplicated malaria must be treated with artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT). Ghana’s primary first-line therapeutic choice is:
  * **Artesunate-Amodiaquine (AA)**
  * *Alternative for patients with known AA intolerance:* Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) or Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHAP).
* **Severe Malaria Protocol:** Patients showing red flags such as continuous vomiting, severe anemia, or altered consciousness must receive immediate intravenous **Artesunate** or intramuscular **Artether** at a district hospital.
* **Preventive Standards:** Continuous distribution of Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs) through schools, and Intermittent Preventive Treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) using Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine (SP) starting from the second trimester.

## Condition: Cholera and Waterborne Diseases

### Profile
Cholera outbreaks in Ghana occur sporadically, typically localized within high-density urban communities in the Greater Accra and Central regions, peaking heavily during the rainy seasons due to drainage vulnerabilities. It is caused by the ingestion of food or water contaminated with *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Sudden onset of severe, painless, profuse watery stools (rice-water stools).
* Frequent projectile vomiting without preceding nausea.
* Rapid dehydration markers: dry tongue, sunken eyes, muscle cramps, and dangerous drops in blood pressure.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Cholera, Vibrio cholerae, Rice-water stool, Acute Watery Diarrhea.
* **Akan (Twi):** Ayamtu neofe (vomiting and stooling simultaneously).
* **Ga:** Musu mli tsaa.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Immediate Home Rehydration:** Do not wait to travel to a clinic. Begin administering **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)** immediately to replace lost fluids and electrolytes.
* **Clinical Management:** Severe dehydration cases must be given aggressive intravenous fluid replacement using **Ringer's Lactate**.
* **Pediatric Zinc Supplementation:** Children under 5 must be given Zinc tablets (10–20 mg daily for 10–14 days) to support intestinal recovery.
* **Public Health Mandate:** Safe disposal of feces, strict handwashing with soap under running water (Veronica Bucket protocol), and boiling or chemically treating water before domestic use.

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

### Profile
Tuberculosis is a major chronic airborne infectious disease in Ghana caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. The Ghana Health Service focuses extensively on identifying missing cases at the community level, particularly among populations with high HIV co-infection rates.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Chronic, hacking cough lasting more than 2 weeks.
* Coughing up phlegm containing streaks of blood (hemoptysis).
* Unexplained weight loss, loss of appetite, persistent night sweats, and low-grade fevers.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Tuberculosis, TB, Pulmonary infection, Mycobacterium.
* **Akan (Twi):** Nsamanwa (the chronic/ancestral cough).
* **Ga:** Tsina gbee.

### National Management Protocols
* **Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course (DOTS):** Ghana utilizes the standard DOTS strategy. Treatment is completely free at all public health facilities.
* **First-Line Drug Regimen:** A standardized 6-month combination course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol, administered under the direct observation of a designated healthcare provider or family treatment supporter.
* **Diagnostic Standard:** Sputum samples must be tested using **GeneXpert** molecular testing equipment where available to verify drug-sensitive vs. multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB) vectors.

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health

### Profile
The Ministry of Health Ghana prioritizes the reduction of maternal mortality via structural initiatives like the Free Maternal Healthcare Policy under the National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS). Preventing postpartum hemorrhage and eclampsia remain top priorities.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs (Danger Metrics)
* **Pregnancy:** Sudden swelling of face and hands, severe headaches, or blurred vision (indicators of gestational hypertension or pre-eclampsia).
* **Post-delivery:** Heavy vaginal bleeding that saturates more than one cloth pad within 30 minutes (postpartum hemorrhage vector).
* **Newborn:** Fast breathing, chest pulling inward, yellowing skin, or refusal to suckle.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Antenatal Care (ANC), Postpartum Hemorrhage, Pre-eclampsia.
* **Akan (Twi):** Awofuo mogya (postpartum bleeding), mogya mmoroso (high blood pressure).

### National Care Guidelines
* **Antenatal Tracking:** Pregnant women are integrated into care loops requiring a minimum of 8 Antenatal Care (ANC) interactions to track blood pressure and receive routine iron/folic acid supplements.
* **Labor Management Protocol:** Active Management of the Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL) using a prophylactic injection of **Oxytocin** (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately after baby delivery to prevent life-threatening maternal hemorrhage.

# Ghana Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

---

## Condition: Malaria

**Profile:**
Malaria is hyper-endemic across all 16 regions of Ghana, accounting for approximately 30% of all outpatient visits and 25% of under-five deaths. It is caused by Plasmodium falciparum and transmitted by infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Children under five, pregnant women, and people living with HIV are at highest risk.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Malaria, malaria fever
- Twi: Hurae, asonson (chills), nwunu

**Symptoms:**
- Shivering chills followed by high fever and sweating
- Intense persistent headache, joint weakness, and severe fatigue
- Nausea, vomiting, bitter taste in the mouth, and loss of appetite
- In infants: unexplained lethargy, hot body, and refusal to breastfeed

**Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Hospital Care:**
- Convulsions or inability to stand
- Persistent vomiting preventing oral medication
- Altered consciousness or extreme weakness
- Severe pallor or yellow eyes (severe anemia or jaundice)

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS — T3 Strategy: Test, Treat, Track):**
- Every suspected case must be confirmed by RDT or microscopy before treatment — never treat on symptoms alone
- First-line treatment: Artesunate-Amodiaquine (AA); alternative: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) or Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHAP) for AA intolerance
- Severe malaria: intravenous Artesunate or intramuscular Artether at district hospital
- Pregnant women: IPTp-SP from the second trimester at every ANC visit
- Prevention: Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs) distributed through schools and ANC clinics; indoor residual spraying in high-burden regions

## Condition: Typhoid Fever

**Profile:**
Typhoid fever is endemic in Ghana, particularly in urban informal settlements in Greater Accra and other cities with unreliable water supply and open food vending. It is caused by Salmonella Typhi and spreads through contaminated food and water.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Typhoid, typhoid fever, enteric fever
- Twi: Typhoid wia (typhoid sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Gradually rising fever — step-wise pattern reaching 39–40°C by end of first week
- Severe persistent headache and general body weakness
- Abdominal pain, bloating, constipation (adults) or diarrhea (children)
- Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
- Faint salmon-colored rash on the trunk in the second week
- Enlarged liver and spleen

**Complications Requiring Emergency Care:**
Intestinal perforation and internal bleeding are life-threatening. Any sudden severe abdominal pain or rapid deterioration requires immediate hospital care.

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS):**
- Diagnosis: Widal test or blood culture
- First-line: Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin
- Drug-resistant cases: Ceftriaxone IV
- Complete the full antibiotic course — stopping early causes relapse
- Prevention: treat all drinking water; wash hands before eating and after toilet use; avoid food from unhygienic vendors

## Condition: Cholera and Waterborne Diseases

**Profile:**
Cholera outbreaks in Ghana occur seasonally, concentrated in high-density urban communities in Greater Accra and the Central Region during the rainy season. Poor drainage and contaminated water sources are the primary drivers.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Cholera, running stomach, acute watery diarrhea
- Twi: Ayamtu neofe (vomiting and stooling simultaneously)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden, painless, profuse watery diarrhea — rice-water appearance
- Frequent projectile vomiting
- Rapid dehydration: dry tongue, sunken eyes, muscle cramps, low blood pressure
- In children: extreme lethargy and cold skin are emergency signs

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Begin ORS immediately and go to a health facility. Do not wait. Cholera can be fatal within hours in children.

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS):**
- Begin ORS immediately at home
- Severe dehydration: intravenous Ringer's Lactate at a health facility
- Children under 5: Zinc supplementation (10–20 mg daily for 10–14 days)
- Antibiotics reserved for severe cases only
- Prevention: boil or chemically treat drinking water; wash hands with soap; use Veronica Bucket handwashing stations where available

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

**Profile:**
Ghana ranks among Africa's high TB burden countries. TB is airborne and closely linked to HIV co-infection, poverty, and overcrowded living conditions. Treatment is completely free at all public health facilities under the DOTS strategy.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: TB, tuberculosis, chest disease
- Twi: Nsamanwa (the chronic cough)

**Symptoms:**
- Chronic cough lasting more than two weeks — may produce blood-stained sputum
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
- Extreme fatigue

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS — DOTS Strategy):**
- Diagnosis: GeneXpert molecular test (preferred) or sputum microscopy
- Treatment: 6-month course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol — free at all public facilities
- Directly Observed Treatment: a healthcare provider or family supporter observes every dose
- Never stop treatment early — creates MDR-TB
- All TB patients tested for HIV; all HIV patients screened for TB

## Condition: Meningitis

**Profile:**
Ghana's Northern, Upper East, and Upper West regions fall within Africa's Meningitis Belt and experience epidemic meningococcal meningitis outbreaks during the dry harmattan season (November–May). Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Meningitis, brain fever
- Twi: Ti yaw a edi kan (severe head pain), kon mu yaw (neck pain)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden severe headache — worst of the person's life
- High fever and stiff neck — unable to touch chin to chest
- Sensitivity to light and loud sounds
- Nausea, vomiting, and confusion
- Non-blanching purple or red rash — medical emergency
- In infants: bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, refusal to feed

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Fever + stiff neck + severe headache = go to hospital immediately. Every hour of delay increases risk of death or permanent disability.

**Prevention:**
MenAfriVac vaccine available in northern regions; routine meningococcal vaccination recommended for children.

## Condition: Malnutrition

**Profile:**
Malnutrition remains a significant problem in Ghana, particularly in the Northern, Upper East, and Upper West regions. Stunting affects approximately 19% of children under five nationally, with rates exceeding 30% in northern regions. It is both caused by and worsens infectious disease.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Malnutrition, kwashiorkor, stunting, wasting
- Twi: Aduan a enni ho (insufficient food), Abofra a onni ahooden (weak child)

**Symptoms:**
- In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones
- MUAC below 11.5 cm = severe acute malnutrition
- Swollen face, feet, and legs (kwashiorkor)
- Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
- Extreme lethargy and irritability
- Persistent infections and slow wound healing

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS):**
- SAM: treat with RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food) at Community-based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) sites
- Do not give regular food to a severely malnourished child without medical guidance
- Prevention: exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; appropriate complementary feeding; routine Vitamin A supplementation and deworming campaigns

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health

**Profile:**
Ghana has made significant progress in reducing maternal mortality under the Free Maternal Healthcare Policy through the National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS). However, postpartum hemorrhage and eclampsia remain leading causes of maternal death. All deliveries should occur in a health facility.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Pregnancy complications, postpartum bleeding, eclampsia
- Twi: Awofuo mogya (postpartum bleeding), mogya mmoroso (high blood pressure in pregnancy)

**Danger Signs During Pregnancy:**
- Sudden swelling of face and hands, severe headache, or blurred vision — signs of pre-eclampsia
- Any vaginal bleeding
- Reduced or absent fetal movement
- Fever with lower abdominal pain

**Danger Signs After Delivery:**
- Heavy vaginal bleeding soaking more than one cloth pad within 30 minutes
- High fever with foul-smelling discharge
- Convulsions

**Danger Signs in Newborns:**
- Fast breathing, chest indrawing, or blue lips
- Yellowing of skin or eyes
- Refusal to breastfeed

**National Care Guidelines (GHS):**
- Minimum 8 ANC contacts at a certified health facility
- Oxytocin (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately after delivery — standard across all GHS facilities
- All deliveries under skilled birth attendance
- Newborn: Vitamin K injection, eye drops, BCG and Hepatitis B vaccines at birth

## Condition: HIV and AIDS

**Profile:**
Ghana has a generalized HIV epidemic with approximately 350,000 people living with HIV. Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) is free at all public health facilities. HIV/TB co-infection is a significant driver of TB deaths.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: HIV, AIDS
- Twi: HIV yadeɛ (HIV sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Early: flu-like illness 2–4 weeks after exposure — fever, rash, swollen lymph nodes
- Latent stage: asymptomatic for years
- Advanced AIDS: rapid weight loss, chronic diarrhea, oral thrush, recurring infections, night sweats

**National Treatment Guidelines (GHS):**
- HIV testing free and confidential at all public health facilities
- ART free and started as soon as possible after diagnosis
- Preferred first-line regimen: Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir (TLD)
- All HIV-positive patients screened for TB at every visit
- PMTCT: all pregnant women tested; HIV-positive mothers receive ART throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding

## Condition: Dengue Fever

**Profile:**
Dengue fever is an emerging concern in Ghana, with cases documented in Greater Accra and Ashanti regions. It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, which bites during the day.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Dengue, dengue fever, breakbone fever
- Twi: Dengue wia (dengue sickness), ache wia (pain sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C) lasting 2–7 days
- Severe headache and pain behind the eyes
- Intense muscle and joint pain
- Nausea, vomiting, and swollen glands
- Rash 3–4 days into the fever
- Severe dengue: bleeding gums, blood in vomit or stool — medical emergency

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care: rest, fluids, paracetamol for fever
- Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen — increase bleeding risk
- Severe dengue requires hospital admission

## Condition: Scabies

**Profile:**
Scabies is common in overcrowded homes and schools in Ghana, particularly in northern regions. It spreads through prolonged skin-to-skin contact and shared bedding.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Scabies
- Twi: Hwam (itch), hwam yadeɛ (itch sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Intense itching, especially at night
- Pimple-like rash with small red bumps and blisters
- Burrow tracks between fingers, wrists, elbows, armpits, and waistline

**Treatment:**
- Permethrin 5% cream to entire body from neck to toes; leave 8–12 hours; wash off
- All household members treated simultaneously
- Wash all bedding and clothing in hot water on treatment day
- Repeat after one week

## Condition: Ringworm (Tinea)

**Profile:**
Ringworm is a very common fungal skin infection in Ghanaian children and adults, spread through direct contact with infected people, animals, or contaminated surfaces.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Ringworm, fungal infection
- Twi: Obi ahokyere (circular skin mark), sikaduro yadeɛ (fungus sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Circular, scaly, ring-shaped patches with raised edges (body)
- Scaly scalp patches with hair loss (common in children)
- Scaling and cracking between toes (athlete's foot)
- Red itchy rash in the groin (jock itch)

**Treatment:**
- Body and foot: topical antifungal cream (clotrimazole or miconazole) for 2–4 weeks
- Scalp: oral griseofulvin — topical creams do not penetrate the scalp
- Keep areas dry; do not share combs, towels, or hats

## Condition: Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

**Profile:**
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It is not contagious. On darker skin tones — as is typical in Ghana — eczema often appears as dark brown, grey, or purple patches rather than the red appearance common in medical textbooks, leading to frequent misdiagnosis.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Eczema, dry skin rash
- Twi: Honam a eye hyew (skin that is dry and itchy)

**Symptoms:**
- Dry, sensitive skin with intense itching — worse at night
- Dark brown, grey, or purple patches on darker skin
- Rough, leathery, or scaly patches
- Common locations: inside elbows, behind knees, hands, feet, face, neck

**Management:**
- Moisturize daily with fragrance-free emollients — shea butter is widely available and effective
- Mild, unscented soap; avoid hot water baths
- Hydrocortisone cream (1%) for flare-ups
- Avoid triggers: harsh soaps, dust, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Seek care if skin becomes warm, weeping, or crusted

## Condition: Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

**Profile:**
Conjunctivitis is common in Ghana, spreading rapidly in schools and households. Outbreaks of viral conjunctivitis occur periodically, particularly following close contact in crowded settings.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Conjunctivitis, pink eye
- Twi: Ani kokoo (red eye)

**Symptoms:**
- Red or pink whites of the eye
- Thick yellow or green discharge crusting the eyelashes on waking
- Increased tearing, burning, and gritty sensation
- Sensitivity to light

**Treatment:**
- Bacterial: chloramphenicol eye drops or ointment
- Viral: resolves on its own in 1–2 weeks — no antibiotic needed
- Clean discharge with clean, damp cloth; do not share towels or pillows
- Wash hands frequently; keep affected children home from school

## Condition: Sickle Cell Disease

**Profile:**
Ghana has one of the highest rates of sickle cell disease in West Africa, with approximately 2% of births affected by sickle cell anaemia. Newborn screening is available at major hospitals. It is a hereditary condition, not infectious.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Sickle cell, sickle cell anaemia
- Twi: Mogya yadeɛ (blood sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Episodes of severe pain (crises) in the chest, abdomen, joints, and bones
- Chronic anemia: fatigue, pale appearance, yellowing of eyes
- Swollen hands and feet in infants
- Frequent infections
- Delayed growth and development
- Stroke risk even in young children

**Management:**
- Daily penicillin for children under 5
- Daily folic acid supplementation
- Hydroxyurea to reduce crisis frequency
- Adequate hydration at all times
- Immediate care for painful crisis, chest pain, or fever
- Know your genotype — newborn screening available at Korle-Bu, Komfo Anokye, and major regional hospitals

## Condition: Monkeypox (Mpox)

**Profile:**
Ghana has documented mpox cases in recent years, predominantly in the southern forest regions. It spreads through close physical contact with an infected person's rash, bodily fluids, or respiratory droplets.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Monkeypox, mpox
- Twi: Mpox yadeɛ (mpox sickness)

**Symptoms:**
- Fever, severe headache, swollen lymph nodes, back pain, and muscle aches
- Rash 1–3 days after fever — begins on face, spreads to palms, soles, and body
- Rash progresses: flat spots → raised bumps → blisters → pustules → scabs
- Lesions are deep-seated, well-defined, and may be painful

**When to Seek Care:**
Unexplained rash with fever and swollen lymph nodes — seek care and avoid close contact with others. Report to Ghana Health Service.

## Condition: Jaundice

**Profile:**
Jaundice — yellowing of the skin and eyes — is a common presenting symptom across Ghana. The most frequent causes are malaria, viral hepatitis (Hepatitis B is highly prevalent in Ghana, with a carrier rate of approximately 12%), sickle cell disease, and liver disease.

**How Ghanaians Describe It:**
- English: Jaundice, yellow eyes
- Twi: Ani tenten (yellow eyes), honam tenten (yellow skin)

**Symptoms:**
- Yellow discoloration of skin and whites of the eyes
- Dark, tea-colored urine
- Pale or clay-colored stools
- Itching of the skin
- Fatigue and abdominal pain

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
- Jaundice in a newborn within 24 hours of birth — urgent phototherapy needed
- Jaundice with fever, severe abdominal pain, or confusion — medical emergency
- Any jaundice lasting more than 2 weeks should be evaluated

# Ghana Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

## Condition: Malaria

### Profile
Malaria remains a hyper-endemic health challenge across all 16 regions of Ghana, accounting for a significant percentage of outpatient clinic visits. It is caused by the *Plasmodium falciparum* parasite and transmitted via the bites of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Shivering chills (*asonson*) followed by elevated body temperature (high fever).
* Intense, persistent headache, joint weakness, and severe bodily fatigue.
* Bitter taste in the mouth, nausea, loss of appetite, and vomiting.
* In infants: unexplained lethargy, hotness of the body, and refusal to breastfeed.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Malaria, Plasmodium falciparum, Anopheles mosquito.
* **Akan (Twi / Fante):** Hurae, asonson, nwunu (chills).
* **Ga:** Atridii.
* **Ewe:** Asrã.

### National Management Protocols
* **Diagnostic Mandate:** The Ghana Health Service strictly enforces the "T3" strategy: **Test, Treat, and Track**. Every suspected malaria case must be confirmed using a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or microscopy before administering drugs.
* **First-Line Intervention:** Confirmed uncomplicated malaria must be treated with artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT). Ghana’s primary first-line therapeutic choice is:
  * **Artesunate-Amodiaquine (AA)**
  * *Alternative for patients with known AA intolerance:* Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) or Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHAP).
* **Severe Malaria Protocol:** Patients showing red flags such as continuous vomiting, severe anemia, or altered consciousness must receive immediate intravenous **Artesunate** or intramuscular **Artether** at a district hospital.
* **Preventive Standards:** Continuous distribution of Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs) through schools, and Intermittent Preventive Treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) using Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine (SP) starting from the second trimester.

## Condition: Cholera and Waterborne Diseases

### Profile
Cholera outbreaks in Ghana occur sporadically, typically localized within high-density urban communities in the Greater Accra and Central regions, peaking heavily during the rainy seasons due to drainage vulnerabilities. It is caused by the ingestion of food or water contaminated with *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Sudden onset of severe, painless, profuse watery stools (rice-water stools).
* Frequent projectile vomiting without preceding nausea.
* Rapid dehydration markers: dry tongue, sunken eyes, muscle cramps, and dangerous drops in blood pressure.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Cholera, Vibrio cholerae, Rice-water stool, Acute Watery Diarrhea.
* **Akan (Twi):** Ayamtu neofe (vomiting and stooling simultaneously).
* **Ga:** Musu mli tsaa.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Immediate Home Rehydration:** Do not wait to travel to a clinic. Begin administering **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)** immediately to replace lost fluids and electrolytes.
* **Clinical Management:** Severe dehydration cases must be given aggressive intravenous fluid replacement using **Ringer's Lactate**.
* **Pediatric Zinc Supplementation:** Children under 5 must be given Zinc tablets (10–20 mg daily for 10–14 days) to support intestinal recovery.
* **Public Health Mandate:** Safe disposal of feces, strict handwashing with soap under running water (Veronica Bucket protocol), and boiling or chemically treating water before domestic use.

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

### Profile
Tuberculosis is a major chronic airborne infectious disease in Ghana caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. The Ghana Health Service focuses extensively on identifying missing cases at the community level, particularly among populations with high HIV co-infection rates.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Chronic, hacking cough lasting more than 2 weeks.
* Coughing up phlegm containing streaks of blood (hemoptysis).
* Unexplained weight loss, loss of appetite, persistent night sweats, and low-grade fevers.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Tuberculosis, TB, Pulmonary infection, Mycobacterium.
* **Akan (Twi):** Nsamanwa (the chronic/ancestral cough).
* **Ga:** Tsina gbee.

### National Management Protocols
* **Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course (DOTS):** Ghana utilizes the standard DOTS strategy. Treatment is completely free at all public health facilities.
* **First-Line Drug Regimen:** A standardized 6-month combination course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol, administered under the direct observation of a designated healthcare provider or family treatment supporter.
* **Diagnostic Standard:** Sputum samples must be tested using **GeneXpert** molecular testing equipment where available to verify drug-sensitive vs. multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB) vectors.

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health

### Profile
The Ministry of Health Ghana prioritizes the reduction of maternal mortality via structural initiatives like the Free Maternal Healthcare Policy under the National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS). Preventing postpartum hemorrhage and eclampsia remain top priorities.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs (Danger Metrics)
* **Pregnancy:** Sudden swelling of face and hands, severe headaches, or blurred vision (indicators of gestational hypertension or pre-eclampsia).
* **Post-delivery:** Heavy vaginal bleeding that saturates more than one cloth pad within 30 minutes (postpartum hemorrhage vector).
* **Newborn:** Fast breathing, chest pulling inward, yellowing skin, or refusal to suckle.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Antenatal Care (ANC), Postpartum Hemorrhage, Pre-eclampsia.
* **Akan (Twi):** Awofuo mogya (postpartum bleeding), mogya mmoroso (high blood pressure).

### National Care Guidelines
* **Antenatal Tracking:** Pregnant women are integrated into care loops requiring a minimum of 8 Antenatal Care (ANC) interactions to track blood pressure and receive routine iron/folic acid supplements.
* **Labor Management Protocol:** Active Management of the Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL) using a prophylactic injection of **Oxytocin** (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately after baby delivery to prevent life-threatening maternal hemorrhage.

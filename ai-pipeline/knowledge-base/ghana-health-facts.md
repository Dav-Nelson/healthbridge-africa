Introduction
Africa faces more health challenges than any other region of the world. Studies have identified seven major conditions as the leading causes of infectious or communicable illness and death in Africa.

The World Health Organization (WHO) reported that these communicable diseases were responsible for substantial years lost in Africans due to ill health or disability that was estimated at 704,765,879 Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) in 2015 alone.

The drivers of the burden of these conditions in Africa are many, including:
* Poverty
* Rampant corruption
* Overpopulation
* Improper sanitation and hygiene
* Poor access to clean water
* Low health literacy
* Poor funding of public health services
* Inadequate health systems infrastructure
* Inadequate service providers

This article will focus on these seven common communicable diseases in Africa, with emphasis on their respective causes and public health control. The diagnosis, symptoms, and treatment of each of the diseases will be the subject of future separate articles.
* Malaria
* Tuberculosis
* HIV/AIDS
* Cholera
* Diarrhea
* Lower Respiratory Tract Infections
* Schistosomiasis

## 1. Malaria

### Key Facts
* Malaria is transmitted through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes carrying different species of Plasmodium parasites in their saliva.
* The majority of malaria cases in Sub-Saharan Africa are caused specifically by the Plasmodium falciparum parasite species.
* In 2023, Africa accounted for 94% of malaria cases and 95% of malaria deaths worldwide.

### Overview
Malaria is a life-threatening, fever-inducing illness transmitted through the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. Infected female mosquitoes carry the Plasmodium parasite in their saliva; when these mosquitoes bite, the parasite enters the bloodstream.

Once the parasites enter the bloodstream, they travel to the liver where they mature, reproduce, and are released back into the bloodstream to propagate the infection. These parasites also infect and destroy human red blood cells. Malaria is especially dangerous for children under the age of five, the elderly, and pregnant women.

According to World Health Organization (WHO) reports, the African region carries a disproportionately high share of the global malaria burden. In 2021, there were 241 million reported cases of malaria worldwide, resulting in 619,000 deaths. As of 2023, estimated malaria cases stood at 263 million globally (compared to 252 million in 2022), while global malaria deaths stood at 597,000. 

In 2023, the African region accounted for 95% of these global cases and 96% of global malaria deaths. Children under the age of five accounted for roughly 76% of all malaria deaths within the region. Within Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), four countries accounted for nearly half of all global malaria deaths in 2021:
* Nigeria (26.6%)
* The Democratic Republic of the Congo (12.3%)
* Uganda (5.1%)
* Mozambique (4.1%)

### Causes of Malaria
Although there are many Plasmodium species, only five cause malaria in humans:
* *Plasmodium falciparum*
* *Plasmodium vivax*
* *Plasmodium ovale*
* *Plasmodium malariae*
* *Plasmodium knowlesi*

*Plasmodium falciparum* and *Plasmodium vivax* are considered the most deadly. Nearly all cases of malaria in SSA are caused by *Plasmodium falciparum*, which is also the most common cause in Southeast Asia, the Eastern Mediterranean, and Western Pacific regions. Comparatively, *Plasmodium vivax* causes 75% of malaria cases in the WHO Region of the Americas (AMR). *Plasmodium knowlesi* rarely occurs in humans and is primarily transmitted among primates rather than via standard mosquito vectors.

### Symptoms
* Cyclic attacks of shivering and chills, followed by high fever and sweating
* Severe anemia
* Jaundice
* Organ failure

## 2. Tuberculosis

### Key Facts
* Tuberculosis is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (MTB).
* Pulmonary tuberculosis (lung infection) is the most common form, though other organs can be affected.
* Africa and especially SSA accounts for a quarter of new tuberculosis cases and nearly 42% of total TB deaths worldwide.

### Overview
Tuberculosis (TB) is one of the oldest and most pervasive infectious diseases in Africa. It is an airborne disease transmitted when a person with active pulmonary TB coughs, sneezes, sings, or spits, allowing another individual to inhale the airborne bacteria. 

When the infection targets the lungs, it is known as pulmonary tuberculosis. However, it can manifest elsewhere. "Tuberculosis can affect other parts of the body such as the spine, the abdomen, the brain, the heart, the kidneys, and the skin. The types of tuberculosis affecting other organs are known as extrapulmonary tuberculosis," notes Dr. Inaku Paul, Medical Officer at the University of Calabar Medical Center.

Individuals with latent tuberculosis do not exhibit symptoms and cannot spread the disease. Conversely, those with active tuberculosis develop symptoms and readily transmit the bacteria. 

According to the WHO, 2.5 million people fell ill with TB in the African region in 2016, accounting for a quarter of new cases globally. That same year, approximately 417,000 people died of TB in the African region out of 1.7 million global deaths. TB frequently presents as a co-infection with HIV/AIDS; individuals living with HIV or those who smoke face a significantly elevated risk of developing active tuberculosis.

### Symptoms
* Persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks
* Chest pain and coughing up blood
* Unexplained weight loss
* Night sweats and fatigue

## 3. HIV/AIDS

### Key Facts
* Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) attacks the body’s immune system, specifically targeting white blood cells known as CD4 cells.
* By destroying CD4 cells, HIV weakens immunity against opportunistic infections (such as TB and fungal infections), severe bacterial conditions, and specific cancers.
* If left untreated, the infection progresses into Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) over a period of 10 to 15 years.
* As of 2018, the African region had the highest global burden, housing roughly 25.7 million of the 37.9 million people living with HIV worldwide.

### Overview
HIV selectively destroys CD4 or T cells, compromising the immune system's capacity to fight off malignancies and opportunistic pathogens. AIDS represents the advanced clinical stage of HIV infection, defined by severe immune damage and the emergence of associated clinical syndromes.

Transmission occurs through direct contact with specific infected bodily fluids:
* Blood
* Semen
* Vaginal secretions
* Breast milk

Common exposure routes include unprotected sexual contact, sharing contaminated needles, and vertical transmission from mother to child during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding. 

The African region accounts for nearly two-thirds of all new global HIV infections. In 2018, approximately 1.1 million individuals were newly infected within the region. In 2016 alone, roughly 24,000 children died from AIDS-related complications in Nigeria.

### Symptoms
* **Stage 1: Acute HIV Infection (Early Stage):** Occurs 2 to 4 weeks post-exposure. Roughly two-thirds of individuals experience flu-like symptoms lasting from a few days to several weeks, including fever, chills, swollen lymph nodes (neck, armpits, groin), red torso rashes, sore throat, painful mouth ulcers, muscle aches, and heavy night sweats.
* **Stage 2: Clinical Latency (Chronic Stage):** The virus reproduces at low levels. Patients are largely asymptomatic. Without antiretroviral therapy (ART), this stage lasts 7 to 10 years. Near the end of this phase, patients may experience persistent lymphadenopathy, mild weight loss, recurrent fevers, and oral thrush.
* **Stage 3: AIDS (Advanced HIV):** Characterized by critical degradation of the immune system. Symptoms include rapid unexplained weight loss (wasting syndrome), chronic diarrhea lasting over a week, drenching night sweats, extensive oral thrush, persistent cough, dyspnea (often due to opportunistic pneumonia), and neurological deficits like memory loss or depression.

## 4. Cholera

### Key Facts
* Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection of the small intestine that can cause fatal dehydration within hours if left untreated.
* Global estimates range from 1.3 to 4.0 million cases and 21,000 to 143,000 deaths annually, with Sub-Saharan Africa bearing the highest burden.
* The primary transmission route is the ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Overview
While the majority of infected individuals experience mild to moderate symptoms, a minority develop acute, severe watery diarrhea. "Cholera is rarely documented in developed urban areas with robust water infrastructure, proper food hygiene, and modern sanitation systems," notes Dr. Adah, Senior Medical Officer at the University of Calabar Medical Center. 

Infected individuals can shed the bacteria in their feces for 1 to 10 days post-infection, often without showing symptoms, contributing to silent environmental contamination. Children and immunocompromised individuals (such as those living with HIV/AIDS) face the highest morbidity and mortality rates. Furthermore, individuals with blood type O are statistically more prone to developing severe clinical manifestations of cholera.

Case fatality rates can fluctuate between 5% and 50% based entirely on the speed of therapeutic intervention. Cholera persists via both sporadic outbreaks and endemic patterns across multiple African nations, including Nigeria, Liberia, Kenya, Tanzania, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and South Sudan. The proportion of patients who die from reported cholera remains higher in Africa than anywhere else in the world.

### Symptoms
* Acute, severe watery diarrhea ("rice-water stools")
* Projectile vomiting
* Severe dehydration and rapid electrolyte loss

## 5. Diarrhea

### Key Facts
* In Africa, diarrheal disease is the second leading cause of death in children under five years old. 
* Globally, there are nearly 1.7 billion cases of childhood diarrheal disease every year, killing around 525,000 children under five annually.
* Diarrheal disease is a primary driver of childhood malnutrition.

### Overview
The WHO defines diarrhea as the passage of three or more loose or watery stools per day (or a frequency exceeding what is normal for the individual). The frequent passage of formed or pasty stools (such as those common in exclusively breastfed infants) does not constitute diarrhea.

The prevalence of childhood diarrheal disease remains elevated in regions like East Africa due to deficits in safe drinking water, basic hygiene, and sanitation infrastructure. 

### Clinical Classification
* **Acute Watery Diarrhea:** Lasts a few days and resolves spontaneously. Typically driven by a transient viral, bacterial, or parasitic infection. In children under five, viral gastroenteritis caused by Rotavirus accounts for 40% of these cases.
* **Acute Bloody Diarrhea (Dysentery):** Characterized by blood in the stool, often accompanying a similar clinical course to watery diarrhea, commonly caused by *Shigella* species.
* **Chronic Diarrhea:** Persists for four weeks or longer. Typically stems from functional gastrointestinal disorders (e.g., Irritable Bowel Syndrome) or structural conditions like Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD).

### Symptoms
* Loose, watery, or abnormally frequent bowel movements
* Abdominal cramping, bloating, or acute pain
* Sudden, severe fecal urgency
* Nausea, vomiting, fever, or mild chills

## 6. Lower Respiratory Tract Infections (LRTI)

### Overview
Lower Respiratory Tract Infections occur within the lungs and lower airways (bronchi and bronchioles), primarily driven by viral or bacterial pathogens transmitted via respiratory droplets or contaminated fomites. Common manifestations include:
* Pneumonia
* Tuberculosis
* Bronchitis
* Bronchiolitis

### Clinical Profiles
* **Pneumonia:** An infection causing inflammation within the pulmonary alveoli (air sacs). In healthy lungs, alveoli fill with air; in pneumonia patients, they fill with pus and cellular debris, restricting oxygen diffusion and making respiration painful. It is primarily bacterial or viral in origin.
* **Bronchitis:** Inflammation of the mucous membranes lining the bronchial tubes, which transport air to and from the lungs. It is common in older children and adults.
* **Bronchiolitis:** Inflammation of the smaller bronchioles, causing swelling and airway obstruction. It predominantly affects infants and toddlers under two years old and is commonly triggered by Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV), Rhinovirus, or Human Metapneumovirus.

LRTI is a leading cause of mortality in children under five globally, with roughly half of these deaths occurring within the African continent.

### Symptoms
* Rapid respiration (tachypnea), wheezing, and stabbing pleuritic chest pain
* High fever, diaphoresis (sweating), and shaking chills (rigors)
* Anorexia, lethargy, and generalized myalgia (muscle aches)

## 7. Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia)

### Key Facts
* Nigeria is the most schistosomiasis-endemic nation in Africa, displaying an overall prevalence of 9.5% with approximately 24 million individuals at risk.
* An estimated 85% of global schistosomiasis cases occur in Africa, where local prevalence rates can exceed 50%.

### Overview
Schistosomiasis is caused by parasitic flatworms known as schistosomes. Infection occurs when larval forms of the parasite (cercariae), released by specific freshwater snail hosts, directly penetrate human skin during contact with contaminated water. Transmission cycles are sustained when infected individuals pass urine or feces containing parasite eggs into freshwater bodies.

### Major Species & Forms
1. **Intestinal Schistosomiasis:** Primarily caused by *Schistosoma mansoni*, *Schistosoma japonicum*, *Schistosoma mekongi*, and *Schistosoma guineensis* (including the related *S. intercalatum*).
2. **Urogenital Schistosomiasis:** Caused by *Schistosoma haematobium*.

The disease predominantly affects rural agricultural and fishing communities. Women performing domestic tasks (such as washing clothes in open water sources) frequently develop female genital schistosomiasis, while children playing in contaminated water run an exceptionally high risk of infection.

## 8. Viral Hemorrhagic Fevers (VHFs)

### Overview
Viral Hemorrhagic Fevers represent severe, acute viral infections capable of causing multi-organ damage and systemic vascular disruption.

### Clinical Profiles
* **Ebola & Marburg Virus Diseases:** Onset is sudden, characterized by acute fever, profound asthenia (weakness), and sore throat. This quickly escalates to severe vomiting, diarrhea, impaired hepatic and renal function, and conspicuous internal and external hemorrhaging.
* **Lassa Fever:** Typically presents with initial pyrexia (fever), malaise, and diffuse muscle pain, which can progress to bleeding from bodily orifices, widespread vascular leakage, and hypovolemic shock.
Warning signs of communicable diseases include sudden fever, persistent cough, extreme fatigue, and gastrointestinal issues. Recognizing these early indicators allows for prompt medical intervention, helping to prevent the spread of contagious infections to others. 
The most common early and systemic warning signs include:
* Fever or Chills: A rise in body temperature is one of the body's primary responses to an infection.
* Fatigue and Muscle Aches: General malaise, lethargy, and widespread muscle or joint pain.

Signs are often grouped by how the disease primarily affects the body: 

1. Respiratory Symptoms
* Persistent or severe coughing, sometimes accompanied by wheezing.
* Sore throat or difficulty swallowing.
* Nasal congestion, sneezing, or a runny nose. 

2. Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* Watery diarrhea or frequent loose stools.
* Severe abdominal cramps or pain.
* Nausea, vomiting, and a sudden loss of appetite. 

3. Skin and Body Indicators
* Unexplained skin rashes, redness, or lesions (e.g., chickenpox or measles).
* Swollen lymph nodes, particularly in the neck, armpits, or groin.
* Unexplained or unintentional weight loss (common in diseases like tuberculosis)

When to Seek Immediate Medical Attention
While many communicable diseases are mild (like the common cold), you should seek professional medical care immediately if you experience the following "red flag" symptoms:
* Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath.
* High, persistent fever that does not respond to medication.
* Confusion, severe headache, or a stiff neck.
* Signs of severe dehydration (e.g., extreme thirst, dry mouth, little to no urination).
* A bluish tint to the lips or face. 

Home remedies cannot cure communicable (infectious) diseases, but they are highly effective at easing symptoms and supporting your immune system while your body fights off the infection. Communicable illnesses range from the common cold to more severe bacterial or viral infections. 
Always consult a healthcare provider for severe symptoms or conditions requiring targeted medical treatment (like antibiotics or antivirals). 

Essential Support Strategies
* Aggressive Hydration: Drink plenty of water, clear broths, or warm lemon water. Fluids thin out mucus, ease congestion, and prevent dehydration caused by fevers. 
* Prioritized Rest: Stay home and sleep as much as possible. Rest allows your immune system to redirect its maximum energy toward fighting off the pathogen.
* 
    * Air Humidification: Use a cool-mist humidifier or take a steamy shower. The added moisture soothes dry, irritated breathing passages and loosens stubborn chest and nasal congestion. 

Science-Backed Symptom Relief
* Saltwater Gargle: Dissolve \(\frac{1}{4}\) to \(\frac{1}{2}\) teaspoon of salt in 8 ounces of warm water. Gargling several times a day temporarily draws out fluid to reduce throat swelling and discomfort. 
* Honey: Consume a spoonful of honey or mix it into warm tea to act as a natural cough suppressant and soothe raw throat tissues. (Safety note: Never give honey to infants under 12 months old due to the risk of botulism). 
* Ginger and Garlic: Brew fresh ginger tea to curb infection-related nausea and muscle pain. Add fresh garlic to meals; it contains allicin, which possesses mild, natural antimicrobial properties. 
* Chicken Soup: Eat warm chicken or bone broth. The heat speeds up nasal clearance, while the nutrients provide easily digestible calories that limit inflammatory white blood cell movement


NON-COMMUNICABLE DISEASES
Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)—primarily cardiovascular diseases, cancers, diabetes, and chronic respiratory diseases—are a rapidly escalating health crisis in Africa. Driven by rising urbanization, dietary shifts, and lifestyle changes, NCDs now account for over a fifth of all deaths in sub-Saharan Africa, placing a heavy burden on developing health systems. 

The Leading NCDs in Africa
* Cardiovascular Disease (CVD): Conditions like hypertension and stroke are leading causes of NCD-related mortality across the continent. In many areas, over a third of adults live with hypertension, but very few have it under control. 
SYMPTOMS:
Heart & Chest
* Chest pain or pressure (Angina): Tightness, heaviness, or discomfort in the chest, sometimes radiating to the neck, jaw, back, or arms.
* Heart palpitations: Sensation of a racing, pounding, or fluttering heartbeat. 

Breathing & Circulation
* Shortness of breath: Breathlessness that occurs during physical activity or even at rest.
* Swelling: Fluid build-up leading to swelling in the legs, ankles, feet, or abdomen.
* Dizziness: Lightheadedness or fainting spells. 
General Signs
* Unusual fatigue: Extreme tiredness or exhaustion, even after minimal exertion.
* Pain in extremities: Cramping, pain, or numbness in the legs or arms, which can indicate peripheral artery disease. 
Note: Symptoms can present differently depending on the individual. Women, for instance, are more likely to experience atypical symptoms like nausea, indigestion, or unexplained anxiety during a heart attack. Some people may have no symptoms at all until a major event like a heart attack or stroke occurs. 

* Cancer: Cancers (including breast, cervical, and prostate) are a leading cause of death in Africa, made worse by delayed diagnoses and limited screening access.

General Signs to Watch For
* Unexplained Weight Loss: Losing 10 pounds (approx. 4.5 kg) or more for no apparent reason.
* Persistent Fatigue: Extreme tiredness that does not improve with a good night's sleep or rest.
* Unexplained Pain: New or worsening pain anywhere in the body that does not go away.
* Fever or Night Sweats: Frequent unexplained fevers or heavy night sweats. 

Changes to Look For
* Skin Changes: New moles, existing moles that change in size/shape/color, or sores that won't heal.
* Lumps or Thickening: Any unusual lump or thickened area that can be felt under the skin.
* Bowel or Bladder Habits: Persistent constipation, diarrhea, or a noticeable change in stool consistency; pain during urination or blood in the urine.
* Coughing or Hoarseness: A persistent, nagging cough or hoarseness that does not improve.
* Bleeding or Bruising: Unexplained bleeding (e.g., blood in stool, coughing up blood, abnormal vaginal bleeding) or easy bruising.
* Eating and Swallowing: Difficulty swallowing, persistent indigestion, or feeling full after eating very little. 
NB: Visit the hospital or clinic as soon as possible. 

* Diabetes: Over 25 million Africans live with diabetes. Without intervention, this number is projected to double. 
* Chronic Respiratory Diseases: Conditions like chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and asthma heavily affect populations, often exacerbated by household air pollution. 

Diabetes symptoms vary based on how much your blood sugar levels are elevated. While Type 1 diabetessymptoms usually develop rapidly and severely, Type 2 diabetes symptoms often progress slowly and may go completely unnoticed for years. 

The "Classic" Three Signs
* Polyuria: Urinating much more frequently than usual, especially at night.
* Polydipsia: Feeling an intense, unquenchable thirst due to dehydration from frequent urination.
* Polyphagia: Experiencing extreme, constant hunger even after consuming food. 

Other Common Symptoms
* Unintended Weight Loss: Dropping weight rapidly without trying, as the body burns fat and muscle for energy.
* Chronic Fatigue: Feeling weak, exhausted, or severely lacking energy because your body cannot properly use glucose.
* Blurred Vision: Temporary changes in eyesight caused by shifting blood sugar levels pulling fluid from the lenses of the eyes.
* Slow-Healing Wounds: Minor cuts, scratches, or bruises that take an unusually long time to heal.
* Frequent Infections: Getting recurring skin, gum, urinary tract, or vaginal yeast infections.
* Neuropathy Signs: Experiencing tingling, numbness, or a burning pain in the hands or feet.
* Darkened Skin: Noticing patches of velvety, dark skin around the neck, armpits, or groin area (primarily in Type 2 or prediabetes

# Kenya Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

## Condition: Malaria

### Profile
Malaria is a major cause of morbidity and mortality in Kenya. Transmission is highly dynamic: it is hyper-endemic near Lake Victoria and the coastal strip, epidemic-prone in the western highlands, and low/seasonal in arid zones. It is caused by *Plasmodium falciparum* and spread by female *Anopheles* mosquitoes.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* High, undulating fever, intense chills (*baridi kali*), and sweating.
* Severe joint pains, generalized muscle aches, and physical weakness.
* Loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, and mild abdominal pain.
* In toddlers: refusal to feed, lethargy, and mild respiratory distress.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Malaria, Plasmodium falciparum, Anopheles, Lake endemic.
* **Swahili (Kiswahili):** Homa ya malaria, baridi kali, kutetemeka, homa kali.

### National Management Protocols
* **Diagnostic Mandate:** The Ministry of Health mandates that **every single fever case** presenting at a healthcare facility must undergo parasitological confirmation via an RDT or microscopy *before* medication is dispensed.
* **First-Line Intervention:** Confirmed uncomplicated malaria must be treated strictly using Artemisinin-based Combination Therapies (ACTs). The primary national first-line drug is:
  * **Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)** (commonly distributed under clinic packs).
* **Vaccine Integration:** Kenya has successfully rolled out the **RTS,S/AS01 malaria vaccine** into routine immunization schedules. It is administered in a 4-dose schedule to infants starting at 6 months of age within high-risk lake-endemic counties (such as Kisumu, Homa Bay, Migori, and Siaya).
* **Severe Malaria Protocol:** Patients showing danger signs (convulsions, altered consciousness, severe anemia) must receive immediate intravenous or intramuscular **Artesunate** for at least 24 hours before switching to full oral ACT courses.

## Condition: Cholera and Acute Watery Diarrhea

### Profile
Cholera outbreaks in Kenya occur periodically, heavily exacerbated by flooding, urban congestion in informal settlements (e.g., parts of Nairobi and Mombasa), and water scarcity in Arid and Semi-Arid Lands (ASAL). It is caused by eating food or drinking water contaminated with *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Sudden, profuse, painless watery diarrhea mimicking "rice-water" appearance.
* Projectile vomiting and rapid muscle cramping due to electrolyte loss.
* Signs of dehydration: Sunken eyes, poor skin turgor, cold extremities, and absence of urination.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Cholera, Vibrio cholerae, Rice-water stool, AWD.
* **Swahili (Kiswahili):** Kipindupindu, kuhara majimaji, kutapika na kuhara.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Immediate Rehydration:** Aggressive fluid management is mandatory. Provide **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)** immediately while organizing transport. For severe dehydration, switch instantly to intravenous **Ringer's Lactate**.
* **Pediatric Zinc Therapy:** Children aged 6 months to 5 years must receive **Zinc supplementation** (20mg daily, 10mg if under 6 months) for 10–14 days to regenerate intestinal mucosal linings.
* **Antibiotic Reserve:** Use antibiotics (Azithromycin or Doxycycline) *only* for patients with severe dehydration to reduce total stool volume and accelerate clearance.

## Condition: Maternal, Neonatal, and Child Health (MNCH)

### Profile
The Kenya Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child and Adolescent Health (RMNCAH) framework prioritizes reducing preventable maternal and infant deaths. Key focuses include tackling maternal hemorrhage, hypertensive disorders (eclampsia), and ensuring safe deliveries under skilled birth attendants.

### High-Risk Danger Signs
* **Maternal:** Severe headache/blurred vision (Pre-eclampsia signals), convulsions, or sudden vaginal bleeding during or after labor.
* **Neonatal:** Fast breathing (over 60 breaths per minute), chest in-drawing, inability to suckle, or cold-to-the-touch skin (neonatal hypothermia/sepsis).

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Antenatal Care (ANC), Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH), Pre-eclampsia, Neonatal Sepsis.
* **Swahili (Kiswahili):** Kliniki ya wajawazito (ANC), kutoka damu baada ya uzazi (PPH), kifafa cha mimba (eclampsia), mtoto kuwa na homa kali.

### National Care Guidelines
* **Linda Mama Initiative Standard:** Pregnant women are integrated into the national care grid to receive comprehensive **Antenatal Care (ANC)**. The MoH aligns with the updated 8-contact model.
* **Active Management of Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL):** Injection of **Oxytocin** (10 IU) within one minute of delivery is the standard prophylactic protocol across all clinics to eliminate Postpartum Hemorrhage.
* **Neonatal Cord Care:** Apply **Chlorhexidine digluconate (7.1% gel)** to the umbilical cord stump immediately after delivery and daily to prevent neonatal tetanus and systemic sepsis infections.

## Condition: HIV and Tuberculosis (TB) Integrated Care

### Profile
Kenya has a high burden of HIV and TB co-infection. Tuberculosis remains a leading cause of death among people living with HIV (PLHIV). The Ministry of Health implements an integrated "two diseases, one patient" care model to screen and manage both concurrently.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Chronic cough lasting more than 2 weeks (often with blood-stained sputum).
* Unexplained weight loss, drenching night sweats, and persistent low-grade fever.
* Swollen lymph nodes (lymphadenopathy).

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** HIV, Tuberculosis (TB), Co-infection, Antiretroviral Therapy (ART).
* **Swahili (Kiswahili):** Kifua kikuu (TB), virusi vya UKIMWI (VVU), jasho jingi usiku.

### National Management Protocols
* **Universal Screening Rule:** Every TB patient must be tested for HIV, and every HIV patient must be screened for TB at every clinical visit using the 4-sign symptom checklist.
* **Diagnostic Standard for TB:** Use **GeneXpert** as the primary diagnostic test rather than traditional sputum microscopy to detect drug-resistant strains (MDR-TB) early.
* **Treatment Sequence:** * For co-infected patients, start standard short-course anti-TB therapy immediately.
  * Initiate Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) using the national preferred regimen (**Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir - TLD**) within 2 to 8 weeks of starting TB treatment.
* **Preventive Therapy:** Provide **Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT)** or shorter Rifapentine-based regimens to all eligible HIV-positive clients without active TB to prevent latent TB activation.

# Kenya Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

---

## Condition: Malaria

**Profile:**
Malaria transmission in Kenya is highly dynamic and geography-dependent. It is hyper-endemic near Lake Victoria and the coastal strip, epidemic-prone in the western highlands, and low-seasonal in arid zones. Plasmodium falciparum causes the majority of disease; P. vivax is present in some regions. Children under five and pregnant women are at highest risk.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Malaria, malaria fever
- Swahili: Homa ya malaria, baridi kali (intense chills), kutetemeka (shivering), homa kali (severe fever)

**Symptoms:**
- High fever, intense chills, and sweating
- Severe joint pains, muscle aches, and physical weakness
- Loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, and mild abdominal pain
- In toddlers: refusal to feed, lethargy, and mild respiratory distress

**Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Hospital Care:**
- Convulsions or inability to stand
- Altered consciousness or extreme weakness
- Persistent vomiting preventing oral medication
- Dark urine or severe pallor

**National Management Protocols (Kenya MoH):**
- Every fever case at a health facility must be confirmed by RDT or microscopy before treatment
- First-line treatment: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)
- Severe malaria: intravenous or intramuscular Artesunate for at least 24 hours before switching to oral ACT
- Vaccine integration: RTS,S/AS01 malaria vaccine rolled into routine immunization in high-risk lake-endemic counties (Kisumu, Homa Bay, Migori, Siaya) — 4 doses starting at 6 months
- Prevention: LLINs; IPTp-SP for pregnant women at every ANC visit; indoor residual spraying in lake-endemic zones

## Condition: Typhoid Fever

**Profile:**
Typhoid fever is endemic in Kenya, with high incidence in Nairobi informal settlements (Kibera, Mathare), Mombasa, and other urban areas with unreliable water supply. It is caused by Salmonella Typhi and spreads through contaminated food and water.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Typhoid, typhoid fever
- Swahili: Homa ya matumbo (stomach fever), typhoid homa

**Symptoms:**
- Gradually rising fever — step-wise pattern reaching 39–40°C by end of first week
- Severe persistent headache and body weakness
- Abdominal pain, bloating, constipation (adults) or diarrhea (children)
- Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
- Faint salmon-colored rash on the trunk in the second week
- Enlarged liver and spleen
- Confusion in severe untreated cases

**Complications Requiring Emergency Care:**
Intestinal perforation and internal bleeding are life-threatening. Any sudden severe abdominal pain or rapid deterioration requires immediate hospital care.

**National Treatment Guidelines (Kenya MoH):**
- Diagnosis: blood culture (gold standard) or Widal test
- First-line: Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin
- MDR cases: Ceftriaxone IV
- Complete the full antibiotic course
- Prevention: treat or boil all drinking water; wash hands; avoid street food from unhygienic vendors

## Condition: Cholera and Acute Watery Diarrhea

**Profile:**
Cholera outbreaks in Kenya are closely linked to flooding, urban informal settlements (Nairobi, Mombasa), and water scarcity in ASAL regions. Outbreaks frequently occur following floods that contaminate water sources.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Cholera, running stomach, acute watery diarrhea
- Swahili: Kipindupindu, kuhara majimaji (watery diarrhea), kutapika na kuhara (vomiting and diarrhea)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden, profuse, painless watery diarrhea — rice-water appearance
- Projectile vomiting and rapid muscle cramping
- Rapid dehydration: sunken eyes, poor skin turgor, cold extremities, no urination

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Begin ORS immediately and go to a health facility without delay. Cholera can be fatal within hours in children.

**National Treatment Guidelines (Kenya MoH):**
- Begin ORS immediately
- Severe dehydration: intravenous Ringer's Lactate
- Children 6 months to 5 years: Zinc supplementation (20 mg daily; 10 mg if under 6 months) for 10–14 days
- Antibiotics (Azithromycin or Doxycycline) for severe dehydration only
- Prevention: boil or treat drinking water; strict handwashing; safe food preparation

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB) and HIV Co-infection

**Profile:**
Kenya has a high burden of HIV and TB co-infection. TB is the leading cause of death among people living with HIV in Kenya. The Ministry of Health implements an integrated "two diseases, one patient" care model. TB services are free at all public health facilities.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: TB, tuberculosis, chest disease
- Swahili: Kifua kikuu (TB — literally "great chest disease"), jasho jingi usiku (excessive night sweating)

**Symptoms:**
- Chronic cough lasting more than two weeks — may produce blood-stained sputum
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
- Swollen lymph nodes

**National Management Protocols (Kenya MoH):**
- Every TB patient must be tested for HIV; every HIV patient screened for TB at every visit using the 4-sign symptom checklist
- Diagnosis: GeneXpert (preferred) to detect drug-resistant strains (MDR-TB)
- Treatment: standard 6-month short-course anti-TB therapy — free at all public facilities
- HIV/TB co-infected patients: start ART within 2–8 weeks of starting TB treatment
- Preferred ART regimen: Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir (TLD)
- Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT) for all eligible HIV-positive clients without active TB

## Condition: Meningitis

**Profile:**
Meningitis in Kenya occurs in two patterns: sporadic bacterial meningitis (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis) throughout the country, and epidemic meningococcal disease in the northern ASAL counties bordering the Meningitis Belt. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Meningitis, brain fever
- Swahili: Uvimbe wa ubongo (brain swelling), maumivu makali ya kichwa (severe headache)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden severe headache — worst of the person's life
- High fever and stiff neck — unable to touch chin to chest
- Sensitivity to light and loud sounds
- Nausea, vomiting, and confusion
- Non-blanching purple or red rash — medical emergency
- In infants: bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, refusal to feed

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Fever + stiff neck + severe headache = go to hospital immediately.

## Condition: Malnutrition

**Profile:**
Malnutrition in Kenya is concentrated in the ASAL counties (Turkana, Mandera, Wajir, Garissa, Marsabit, Isiolo) where drought and food insecurity are chronic. Nationally, approximately 26% of children under five are stunted and 4% suffer acute wasting — with rates in ASAL counties significantly higher.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Malnutrition, wasting, stunting
- Swahili: Utapiamlo (malnutrition), kudhoofika (wasting/weakness), mtoto mwenye njaa (hungry child)

**Symptoms:**
- In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones
- MUAC below 11.5 cm = severe acute malnutrition
- Swollen face, feet, and legs (kwashiorkor)
- Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
- Extreme lethargy and irritability
- Persistent infections and slow wound healing

**National Treatment Guidelines (Kenya MoH):**
- SAM: RUTF treatment at Community-Based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) sites and stabilization centers
- Do not give regular food to a severely malnourished child without medical guidance
- Prevention: exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; appropriate complementary feeding; Vitamin A supplementation; deworming; Kenya's Inua Jamii cash transfer program for food-insecure households

## Condition: Maternal, Neonatal, and Child Health (MNCH)

**Profile:**
Kenya's RMNCAH (Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child and Adolescent Health) framework prioritizes reducing preventable maternal and neonatal deaths. The Linda Mama initiative provides free maternity services at public facilities. Postpartum hemorrhage and eclampsia remain leading causes of maternal death.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Pregnancy complications, postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia
- Swahili: Kliniki ya wajawazito (antenatal clinic), kutoka damu baada ya uzazi (postpartum bleeding), kifafa cha mimba (eclampsia)

**Danger Signs During Pregnancy:**
- Severe headache or blurred vision — signs of pre-eclampsia
- Convulsions (eclampsia)
- Sudden vaginal bleeding
- Reduced or absent fetal movement
- Fever with lower abdominal pain

**Danger Signs After Delivery:**
- Heavy vaginal bleeding
- High fever with foul-smelling discharge
- Convulsions

**Danger Signs in Newborns:**
- Fast breathing (over 60 breaths per minute) or chest indrawing
- Inability to suckle
- Cold skin (neonatal hypothermia)
- Yellowing within 24 hours of birth

**National Care Guidelines (Kenya MoH — Linda Mama Standard):**
- Minimum 8 ANC contacts
- Oxytocin (10 IU intramuscularly) within one minute of delivery — Active Management of Third Stage of Labour (AMTSL)
- Chlorhexidine digluconate (7.1% gel) on umbilical cord stump daily to prevent neonatal sepsis
- All deliveries under skilled birth attendance at a health facility
- Newborn: Vitamin K, eye drops, BCG and Hepatitis B vaccines at birth

## Condition: HIV and AIDS

**Profile:**
Kenya has approximately 1.4 million people living with HIV. The national ART program — one of the largest in Africa — provides free treatment at public health facilities. Western Kenya (Nyanza region) carries a disproportionate share of the national burden.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: HIV, AIDS
- Swahili: VVU (Virusi vya UKIMWI — HIV), UKIMWI (AIDS)

**Symptoms:**
- Early: flu-like illness 2–4 weeks after exposure
- Latent stage: asymptomatic for years
- Advanced AIDS: rapid weight loss, chronic diarrhea, oral thrush, recurring infections, night sweats

**National Treatment Guidelines (Kenya MoH/NACC):**
- HIV testing free and confidential at all public facilities
- ART free and initiated as soon as possible: preferred regimen TLD (Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir)
- All HIV patients screened for TB at every visit
- PMTCT: all pregnant women tested; HIV-positive mothers on ART throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding

## Condition: Dengue Fever

**Profile:**
Dengue fever is endemic in Kenya's coastal counties (Mombasa, Kilifi, Kwale) and has been documented in Nairobi. It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, which bites during the day.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Dengue, dengue fever, breakbone fever
- Swahili: Homa ya dengue, maumivu ya mifupa (bone pain), homa ya mbu wa mchana (daytime mosquito fever)

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C) lasting 2–7 days
- Severe headache and pain behind the eyes
- Intense muscle and joint pain
- Nausea, vomiting, and swollen glands
- Rash 3–4 days into the fever
- Severe dengue: bleeding gums, blood in vomit or stool — medical emergency

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care: rest, fluids, paracetamol
- Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen
- Severe dengue requires hospital admission

## Condition: Scabies

**Profile:**
Scabies is common in overcrowded urban informal settlements (Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru) and in rural Kenya. It spreads through prolonged skin-to-skin contact and shared bedding.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Scabies, itch mite
- Swahili: Upele wa kuudhi (irritating rash), mwasho wa usiku (night itching)

**Symptoms:**
- Intense itching, especially at night
- Pimple-like rash with small bumps and blisters
- Burrow tracks between fingers, wrists, elbows, armpits, and waistline

**Treatment:**
- Permethrin 5% cream to entire body from neck to toes; leave 8–12 hours; wash off
- All household members treated simultaneously
- Wash all bedding and clothing in hot water
- Repeat after one week

## Condition: Ringworm (Tinea)

**Profile:**
Ringworm is a very common fungal skin infection in Kenyan children and adults, spread through direct contact with infected people, animals, or soil.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Ringworm, fungal infection
- Swahili: Kutu (fungal infection), upele wa mviringo (circular rash)

**Symptoms:**
- Circular, scaly, ring-shaped patches with raised edges (body)
- Scaly scalp patches with hair loss (common in children)
- Scaling and cracking between toes (athlete's foot)
- Red itchy rash in groin (jock itch)

**Treatment:**
- Body and foot: topical antifungal cream for 2–4 weeks
- Scalp: oral griseofulvin
- Keep areas dry; do not share combs, towels, or hats

## Condition: Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

**Profile:**
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It is not contagious. On darker skin tones — as is typical in Kenya — eczema often appears as dark brown, grey, or purple patches rather than red, leading to frequent misdiagnosis or delayed treatment.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Eczema, dry skin
- Swahili: Ngozi kavu (dry skin), upele wa ngozi (skin rash)

**Symptoms:**
- Dry, sensitive skin with intense itching — worse at night
- Dark brown, grey, or purple patches on darker skin
- Rough, leathery patches
- Common locations: inside elbows, behind knees, hands, feet, face, neck

**Management:**
- Moisturize daily with fragrance-free emollients
- Mild unscented soap; avoid hot water baths
- Hydrocortisone cream (1%) for flare-ups
- Avoid triggers: harsh soaps, dust, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Seek care if skin becomes warm, weeping, or crusted

## Condition: Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

**Profile:**
Conjunctivitis is common in Kenya, particularly in schools and households. Trachoma — a chronic form of bacterial conjunctivitis caused by Chlamydia trachomatis — is a leading cause of preventable blindness in Kenya's ASAL counties and is targeted by the national trachoma elimination programme.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Conjunctivitis, pink eye
- Swahili: Macho mekundu (red eyes), macho ya maumivu (painful eyes)

**Symptoms:**
- Red or pink whites of the eye
- Thick yellow or green discharge crusting eyelashes on waking
- Increased tearing, burning, and gritty sensation
- Sensitivity to light
- In trachoma: repeated infections leading to scarring and eventual blindness if untreated

**Treatment:**
- Bacterial (including trachoma): chloramphenicol or tetracycline eye ointment; azithromycin for trachoma
- Viral: resolves on its own in 1–2 weeks
- Clean discharge with clean, damp cloth; do not share towels or pillows
- Mass Drug Administration (azithromycin) for trachoma in endemic ASAL counties

## Condition: Jaundice

**Profile:**
Jaundice in Kenya is most commonly caused by malaria, viral hepatitis (Hepatitis B and E), sickle cell disease, and liver disease. Neonatal jaundice is a significant cause of newborn brain damage and death if not treated promptly.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Jaundice, yellow eyes
- Swahili: Manjano ya macho (yellow eyes), ngozi ya manjano (yellow skin)

**Symptoms:**
- Yellow discoloration of skin and whites of the eyes
- Dark, tea-colored urine
- Pale or clay-colored stools
- Itching, fatigue, and abdominal pain

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
- Jaundice in a newborn within 24 hours of birth — requires urgent phototherapy
- Jaundice with fever, severe abdominal pain, or confusion — medical emergency
- Any jaundice lasting more than 2 weeks should be evaluated

## Condition: Sickle Cell Disease

**Profile:**
Sickle cell disease is prevalent in western Kenya (Nyanza and Western regions), where carrier rates are among the highest in the country due to the historical selection pressure of malaria. Newborn screening is available at major facilities.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Sickle cell, sickle cell anaemia
- Swahili: Ugonjwa wa seli mundu (sickle cell disease), damu mbaya (bad blood — colloquial)

**Symptoms:**
- Episodes of severe pain in chest, abdomen, joints, and bones
- Chronic anemia: fatigue, pale appearance, yellowing of eyes
- Swollen hands and feet in infants
- Frequent infections
- Delayed growth
- Stroke risk in young children

**Management:**
- Daily penicillin for children under 5
- Daily folic acid
- Hydroxyurea to reduce crisis frequency
- Adequate hydration
- Immediate care for painful crisis, chest pain, or fever
- Know your genotype — newborn screening at Kenyatta National Hospital and major regional facilities

## Condition: Monkeypox (Mpox)

**Profile:**
Kenya has documented mpox cases, with proximity to high-burden DRC and Uganda increasing risk. Surveillance is conducted by the Kenya MoH in collaboration with WHO.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Monkeypox, mpox
- Swahili: Ugonjwa wa ndui ya tumbili (monkeypox disease)

**Symptoms:**
- Fever, severe headache, swollen lymph nodes, back pain, muscle aches
- Rash 1–3 days after fever — begins on face, spreads to palms, soles, and body
- Rash progresses: flat spots → raised bumps → blisters → pustules → scabs

**When to Seek Care:**
Unexplained rash with fever and swollen lymph nodes — seek care and avoid close contact. Report to Kenya MoH.

## Condition: Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia)

**Profile:**
Schistosomiasis is endemic in Kenya's Lake Victoria basin (Kisumu, Siaya, Homa Bay, Migori) and in irrigation schemes along the Tana River. Communities with regular contact with freshwater — fishing, bathing, farming — are at highest risk. Kenya accounts for a significant share of East Africa's schistosomiasis burden.

**How Kenyans Describe It:**
- English: Bilharzia, schistosomiasis
- Swahili: Kichocho (bilharzia — the common Swahili term), damu katika mkojo (blood in urine)

**Symptoms:**
- Urogenital form (S. haematobium): blood in urine (hematuria), bladder pain, frequent urination — can lead to bladder cancer in long-term untreated cases
- Intestinal form (S. mansoni): abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, enlarged liver and spleen
- Swimmer's itch: itchy rash shortly after water exposure — early sign

**Treatment:**
- Praziquantel (single oral dose) — safe, effective, and free during Mass Drug Administration campaigns
- Annual MDA in endemic lake and river communities
- Prevention: avoid swimming or wading in Lake Victoria and other freshwater bodies in endemic areas; use treated water for bathing where possible

# Ethiopia Health Facts

## Introduction

Ethiopia, with a population exceeding 126 million people, is the second most populous country in Africa and carries one of the continent's heaviest disease burdens. Despite significant progress made under the government's Health Extension Program (HEP) — one of the most ambitious community health initiatives in Sub-Saharan Africa — Ethiopia continues to grapple with a dual burden of widespread communicable diseases and a rapidly growing prevalence of non-communicable conditions.

The World Health Organization (WHO) reported that communicable diseases were responsible for substantial years lost in Africans due to ill health or disability, estimated at 704,765,879 Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) in 2015 alone. Ethiopia accounts for a disproportionate share of this burden, particularly in rural lowland areas where access to healthcare remains severely limited.

The drivers of the disease burden in Ethiopia are many, including:
* Poverty, with approximately 23% of the population living below the national poverty line
* Poor access to safe drinking water, particularly in rural and pastoral communities
* Inadequate sanitation and open defecation practices in rural areas
* Recurring droughts, floods, and climate shocks that trigger outbreaks and food insecurity
* Low health literacy, especially among women and rural communities
* Internal displacement and large refugee populations
* Shortage of trained healthcare workers outside major urban centers
* Cultural and traditional health-seeking barriers

This document focuses on the major communicable diseases, nutritional conditions, and Ethiopia-specific illnesses affecting the population, with emphasis on their causes, symptoms, public health significance, and control strategies. Local names in **Amharic** (አማርኛ) and **Afaan Oromoo** are noted throughout to support multilingual health communication.

The conditions covered are:
* Malaria
* Tuberculosis
* Cholera
* Malnutrition
* Typhoid Fever
* Hypertension
* Common Childhood Illnesses
* Trachoma
* Dengue Fever


## 1. Malaria
**Amharic:** ወባ (*Weba*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Busaa*

### Key Facts
* Malaria is the leading cause of outpatient visits and one of the top causes of death in Ethiopia, with an estimated 3.8 million clinical cases reported annually.
* Unlike much of Sub-Saharan Africa where *Plasmodium falciparum* dominates overwhelmingly, Ethiopia has a mixed burden: approximately 60% of cases are caused by *P. falciparum* and 40% by *Plasmodium vivax*, a uniquely high proportion of *P. vivax* for the region.
* Transmission is highly seasonal and altitude-dependent, with the majority of cases occurring in lowland areas below 2,000 meters following the rainy seasons (June–September and February–April).
* Ethiopia accounts for approximately 10% of all malaria cases in Sub-Saharan Africa.

### Overview
Malaria is a life-threatening, fever-inducing illness transmitted through the bite of an infected female *Anopheles* mosquito. When an infected mosquito bites a person, *Plasmodium* parasites in the mosquito's saliva enter the bloodstream, travel to the liver, and mature before re-entering the bloodstream to infect and destroy red blood cells, causing the characteristic cycles of fever, chills, and sweating.

Ethiopia's malaria epidemiology is distinct from the broader Sub-Saharan African pattern. The unusually high prevalence of *P. vivax* — which can form dormant liver stages (hypnozoites) capable of causing relapses months to years after the initial infection — complicates treatment and elimination efforts. According to the Federal Ministry of Health of Ethiopia (FMoH), malaria remains endemic in approximately 75% of Ethiopian territory, putting an estimated 60 million people at risk.

High-risk zones include the lowlands of Oromia (particularly Jimma, Ilubabor, Borena, and West Hararghe zones), Benishangul-Gumuz, Gambela, the Southern Nations, Nationalities, and Peoples' Region (SNNPR), and parts of the Afar and Somali regions. Highland populations above 2,000 meters, while historically protected, are increasingly at risk due to climate change-driven shifts in mosquito distribution.

Children under five and pregnant women carry the greatest risk of severe disease and mortality. Severe malaria caused by *P. falciparum* can progress to cerebral malaria, severe anemia, respiratory distress, and multi-organ failure within 24 hours if left untreated.

### Causes of Malaria
Although there are many *Plasmodium* species, only five cause malaria in humans:
* *Plasmodium falciparum*
* *Plasmodium vivax*
* *Plasmodium ovale*
* *Plasmodium malariae*
* *Plasmodium knowlesi*

In Ethiopia, *Plasmodium falciparum* and *Plasmodium vivax* are the two species responsible for virtually all cases. *P. falciparum* causes the most severe and rapidly fatal disease. *P. vivax*, while generally less lethal in the short term, causes significant morbidity through relapses and contributes to chronic anemia, particularly in children and pregnant women.

### Symptoms
* Cyclic attacks of shivering and chills, followed by high fever (38.5°C or above) and sweating
* Severe headache, muscle pain, and generalized fatigue
* Nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite
* Severe anemia (pallor, weakness, rapid heartbeat)
* Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes)
* In severe *P. falciparum* cases: convulsions, altered consciousness, respiratory distress, and organ failure


## 2. Tuberculosis (TB)
**Amharic:** ነቀርሳ (*Nekersa*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Dhukkuba Sombaa* (literally "lung disease")

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia ranks among the 30 high TB burden countries globally, as classified by the WHO.
* The estimated TB incidence in Ethiopia is approximately 140 cases per 100,000 population annually, translating to roughly 165,000 new cases each year.
* Ethiopia accounts for approximately 3.6% of the global TB burden and has one of the highest rates of TB/HIV co-infection in East Africa, with HIV co-infection present in roughly 10–12% of TB patients.
* Drug-resistant TB (DR-TB), including Multi-Drug Resistant TB (MDR-TB), is an increasingly serious concern.

### Overview
Tuberculosis (TB) is one of the oldest and most pervasive infectious diseases in Ethiopia. It is an airborne disease caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (MTB), transmitted when a person with active pulmonary TB coughs, sneezes, sings, or spits, releasing airborne bacteria that another individual inhales.

When the infection targets the lungs, it is known as pulmonary tuberculosis, which is both the most common and most contagious form. However, TB bacteria can spread through the bloodstream to affect virtually any organ. "Tuberculosis can affect other parts of the body such as the spine, the abdomen, the brain, the heart, the kidneys, and the skin. These forms are known as extrapulmonary tuberculosis," as documented in clinical guidelines from the Ethiopian Federal Ministry of Health.

Ethiopia's TB burden is exacerbated by several country-specific factors: overcrowded living conditions in urban slums and internally displaced persons (IDP) camps, high rates of malnutrition weakening immune defenses, widespread tobacco use and indoor cooking smoke exposure, and the significant overlap between TB and HIV/AIDS. Individuals with latent TB infection do not exhibit symptoms and cannot spread the disease; however, those with active TB readily transmit the bacteria and require immediate isolation and treatment.

The Ethiopian government delivers TB services free of charge at all public health facilities through the WHO-recommended DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) strategy, implemented by Health Extension Workers (HEWs) at the community level.

### Causes of Tuberculosis
TB in Ethiopia is caused almost exclusively by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. A small proportion of cases, particularly in rural communities with close cattle contact, are caused by *Mycobacterium bovis* transmitted through the consumption of unpasteurized milk and raw meat — a practice common in Ethiopian food culture (e.g., consumption of *kitfo*, raw minced beef).

### Symptoms
* Persistent cough lasting more than two weeks, which may produce blood-stained sputum
* Chest pain and difficulty breathing
* Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
* Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
* Extreme fatigue and general weakness
* In extrapulmonary TB: swollen lymph nodes, back pain (spinal TB), or neurological symptoms (TB meningitis)


## 3. Cholera
**Amharic:** ኮሌራ (*Kolera*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Koleeraa*

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia experiences recurrent cholera outbreaks, particularly in drought-affected regions, flood-prone lowlands, and urban informal settlements with inadequate water and sanitation infrastructure.
* The Afar, Somali, and Oromia regions bear the greatest cholera burden within Ethiopia, with outbreaks frequently crossing borders from neighboring Somalia, South Sudan, and Kenya.
* Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection that can cause fatal dehydration within hours if untreated; case fatality rates in poorly resourced settings can reach 25–50% without prompt oral or intravenous rehydration.
* The primary transmission route is ingestion of food or water contaminated with *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Overview
Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection of the small intestine caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, primarily serogroups O1 and O139. While the majority of infected individuals experience mild to moderate symptoms or remain entirely asymptomatic, a minority develop the sudden-onset profuse watery diarrhea that constitutes the classic severe presentation capable of causing death within hours.

In Ethiopia, cholera outbreaks are closely linked to humanitarian crises. Recurring droughts force populations into densely crowded emergency settlements where clean water is scarce, while flooding contaminates water sources with sewage and fecal matter. Large-scale population displacements — whether from conflict, drought, or flooding — consistently create conditions conducive to rapid cholera spread. According to the WHO Ethiopia Country Office, the country has experienced repeated large-scale outbreaks, with the 2022–2023 outbreak affecting multiple regions simultaneously and resulting in thousands of cases and deaths.

Infected individuals shed *Vibrio cholerae* bacteria in their feces for one to ten days post-infection, often without showing symptoms themselves, silently contaminating shared water sources. Children under five and immunocompromised individuals, including those living with HIV/AIDS, face the highest risk of severe dehydration and death. Individuals with blood type O are statistically more prone to developing severe clinical manifestations of cholera.

### Causes of Cholera
Cholera is caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*. The pathogen releases a potent enterotoxin in the small intestine that disrupts the normal absorption of water and electrolytes, causing the body to rapidly lose massive amounts of fluid. Transmission routes in Ethiopia include:
* Consumption of water from contaminated rivers, ponds, or unprotected wells
* Consumption of raw or undercooked food, particularly fish and shellfish from contaminated water bodies
* Person-to-person spread via fecal-oral route in crowded settings with poor sanitation
* Traditional funeral practices involving washing of deceased bodies

### Symptoms
* Acute, sudden-onset profuse watery diarrhea with a characteristic "rice-water" appearance (pale, watery stool with a fishy odor)
* Projectile, effortless vomiting
* Severe, rapid dehydration: sunken eyes, dry mouth, decreased skin elasticity, no urine output
* Muscle cramps, particularly in the legs
* Rapid, weak pulse and low blood pressure in severe cases
* Without treatment, progression to hypovolemic shock and death


## 4. Malnutrition
**Amharic:** ምግብ እጦት (*Migib Ithot*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Gad-dhabbuu*

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia has one of the highest rates of childhood stunting (chronic malnutrition) in the world, with approximately 37% of children under five affected — among the ten highest rates globally.
* Acute malnutrition (wasting) affects approximately 9% of children under five nationally, rising to over 20% in drought-affected areas of the Somali, Afar, and parts of the Oromia regions.
* Malnutrition is an underlying contributing factor in an estimated 45% of all child deaths in Ethiopia.
* Micronutrient deficiencies are widespread: iron deficiency anemia affects approximately 44% of children under five and 17% of women of reproductive age; Vitamin A deficiency and iodine deficiency disorders are also prevalent.

### Overview
Malnutrition in Ethiopia encompasses a spectrum from acute life-threatening wasting to chronic stunting, and from undernutrition in food-insecure rural areas to emerging urban overnutrition. The condition reflects the intersection of food insecurity, infectious disease burden, poor infant feeding practices, and structural poverty.

Ethiopia's nutritional crisis is both chronic and cyclical. Recurring droughts — intensified by El Niño climate patterns and exacerbated by the 2020–2022 Tigray conflict — have created acute nutritional emergencies affecting millions. According to UNICEF Ethiopia and the Federal Ministry of Health, over 3 million children required treatment for acute malnutrition in 2023, with the Somali, Afar, Oromia, Tigray, and Amhara regions most severely affected.

Malnutrition operates in a destructive cycle with infectious diseases that is particularly severe in Ethiopia: malaria, diarrhea, and TB all deplete nutritional reserves, while malnutrition itself undermines immune defenses, making children more vulnerable to severe infections. Stunting — low height for age caused by chronic undernutrition beginning in utero and continuing through the first two years of life — causes irreversible impairment to physical growth and cognitive development, with lifelong consequences for educational attainment and economic productivity.

### Clinical Classifications
* **Stunting (Chronic Malnutrition):** Low height-for-age ratio indicating sustained nutritional deprivation over months or years, typically beginning before birth. Affects approximately 37% of Ethiopian children under five.
* **Wasting (Acute Malnutrition):** Low weight-for-height ratio indicating recent, rapid weight loss. Classified as Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM) or Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM). SAM is immediately life-threatening.
* **Underweight:** Low weight-for-age, reflecting a combination of stunting and wasting.
* **Micronutrient Deficiencies:** Inadequate intake or absorption of vitamins and minerals including iron (causing anemia), Vitamin A (impairing immunity and vision), iodine (causing goiter and intellectual impairment), and zinc.

### Symptoms
* In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones, severely reduced mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC)
* Bilateral pitting edema (swollen feet, legs, and face) in kwashiorkor-type severe acute malnutrition
* Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
* Extreme lethargy, irritability, and loss of interest in surroundings
* Persistent infections and delayed wound healing
* In adults: fatigue, weakness, and inability to perform daily activities
* Night blindness (early symptom of Vitamin A deficiency)


## 5. Typhoid Fever
**Amharic:** ታይፎይድ ወባ (*Tayfoid Weba*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Dhukkuba Taayifoyidii*

### Key Facts
* Typhoid fever is endemic in Ethiopia, particularly in urban and peri-urban areas with inadequate water supply, poor sanitation, and high population density.
* The estimated incidence of typhoid in Ethiopia is approximately 45 cases per 100,000 population annually, with rates significantly higher in urban slums.
* Ethiopia experiences typhoid outbreaks in schools, internally displaced persons camps, and informal urban settlements following floods that contaminate water supplies.
* Drug resistance to commonly used antibiotics — including ampicillin, chloramphenicol, and co-trimoxazole — is a growing concern in Ethiopia, with multidrug-resistant (MDR) *Salmonella typhi* increasingly reported.

### Overview
Typhoid fever is a systemic bacterial infection caused by *Salmonella enterica* serovar Typhi (*S. typhi*), transmitted exclusively through the fecal-oral route via ingestion of food or water contaminated with the feces or urine of an infected person or chronic carrier. A closely related organism, *Salmonella paratyphi*, causes paratyphoid fever, a clinically similar but generally milder illness.

In Ethiopia, typhoid flourishes in densely populated urban neighborhoods where treated piped water is unavailable or intermittently supplied, residents rely on shared water sources susceptible to fecal contamination, and open food vending is common. Addis Ababa, Dire Dawa, Hawassa, and Bahir Dar consistently record high typhoid caseloads. School children and young adults aged 5–25 years are disproportionately affected due to higher rates of consumption of street food and untreated water.

The disease progresses in a characteristic step-wise pattern over one to four weeks. Without appropriate antibiotic treatment, complications can be severe and life-threatening, including intestinal hemorrhage (bleeding), intestinal perforation (a hole in the bowel wall requiring emergency surgery), and septicemia (blood poisoning). Approximately 2–5% of recovered typhoid patients become chronic asymptomatic carriers, continuing to shed the bacteria for months to years and serving as persistent community reservoirs.

### Causes of Typhoid Fever
Typhoid is caused exclusively by *Salmonella Typhi*. After ingestion, the bacteria breach the intestinal wall, enter the lymphatic system, and spread through the bloodstream to the liver, spleen, gallbladder, and bone marrow, where they reproduce and trigger the systemic illness. Common transmission routes in Ethiopia include:
* Drinking untreated or inadequately treated water from wells, rivers, or unreliable piped supplies
* Consuming raw vegetables irrigated with wastewater
* Eating food prepared by asymptomatic carriers with poor hand hygiene
* Consuming unpasteurized dairy products

### Symptoms
* Gradually rising, sustained fever that increases in a characteristic step-wise pattern, reaching 39–40°C by the end of the first week
* Severe, persistent headache and generalized malaise
* Relative bradycardia (heart rate lower than expected for the degree of fever)
* Abdominal pain, bloating, and either constipation (more common in adults) or diarrhea (more common in children)
* Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
* Rose spots: faint, salmon-colored rash on the trunk appearing in the second week (visible in lighter-skinned individuals)
* Hepatosplenomegaly (enlarged liver and spleen)
* Altered mental status and confusion ("typhoid state") in severe untreated cases


## 6. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)
**Amharic:** የደም ግፊት (*Yedem Gifit*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Dhiibbaa Dhiigaa Olka'aa*

### Key Facts
* Hypertension is the most common non-communicable disease (NCD) in Ethiopia and a leading risk factor for stroke, heart attack, heart failure, and kidney failure.
* Population-based studies estimate hypertension prevalence at 20–28% among Ethiopian adults, with a large proportion — estimated at over 60% — unaware of their diagnosis.
* Hypertension is increasingly prevalent in both urban and rural Ethiopia, driven by dietary patterns, physical inactivity, rising obesity rates, and chronic stress.
* Ethiopia's health system has historically prioritized communicable diseases, leaving hypertension severely underdiagnosed, undertreated, and uncontrolled in the majority of affected individuals.

### Overview
Hypertension, defined as a sustained blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or above, is the world's leading preventable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and premature death. It is widely called the "silent killer" because it causes no obvious symptoms for years or decades while silently damaging blood vessels, the heart, kidneys, brain, and eyes.

In Ethiopia, hypertension represents a rapidly escalating public health emergency. Urbanization is driving a nutrition and lifestyle transition: increasing consumption of salt-heavy foods (including injera with salt-rich stews), sedentary urban occupations, rising tobacco and alcohol use (including traditional beverages such as *tella*, *tej*, and *areke*), and chronic psychosocial stress related to poverty, displacement, and conflict all contribute to the growing burden. A nationally representative study published in 2019 found that hypertension affected 1 in 4 Ethiopian adults, with higher rates in urban areas (approximately 31%) compared to rural settings (approximately 18%).

Particularly concerning is the role of *khat* (Catha edulis) chewing, a stimulant plant widely used in Ethiopia — particularly in Oromia, Harari, and Dire Dawa — which has been associated with acute and chronic blood pressure elevation, cardiac arrhythmias, and increased stroke risk. Despite the massive burden, the vast majority of hypertensive Ethiopians are undiagnosed, and among those diagnosed, medication adherence is poor due to cost, drug unavailability, and low health literacy regarding the chronic, asymptomatic nature of the condition.

### Causes and Risk Factors
Hypertension in Ethiopia results from a combination of modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors:
* **Dietary factors:** High salt intake through *injera* condiments and processed foods; low potassium intake (insufficient fruit and vegetable consumption)
* **Substance use:** Khat chewing (widespread), alcohol consumption (traditional and commercial), tobacco use
* **Physical inactivity:** Increasingly sedentary lifestyles in urban areas
* **Obesity and overweight:** Rapidly rising in urban populations
* **Chronic stress and psychosocial factors:** Poverty, conflict, displacement
* **Non-modifiable factors:** Older age, male sex (higher rates in men under 65), family history, and ethnicity

### Symptoms
Hypertension is typically asymptomatic until complications develop. When symptoms occur, they may include:
* Persistent headache, particularly at the back of the head, typically worse in the morning
* Dizziness, lightheadedness, or episodes of blurred vision
* Nosebleeds (epistaxis) in severe cases
* Shortness of breath and chest discomfort
* Palpitations (awareness of irregular or forceful heartbeat)

**Hypertensive crisis** (blood pressure above 180/120 mmHg) is a medical emergency presenting with: severe headache, confusion, visual disturbances, chest pain, and shortness of breath — requiring immediate hospital care.


## 7. Common Childhood Illnesses
**Amharic:** የሕፃናት በሽታዎች (*Yehitsanat Beshtawoch*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Dhukkuboota Daa'immanii*

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia has one of the highest rates of child mortality from preventable causes in the world, though under-five mortality has declined substantially — from 204 per 1,000 live births in 1990 to approximately 47 per 1,000 live births in 2022.
* Pneumonia, diarrheal disease, and malaria together account for the majority of under-five deaths in Ethiopia.
* Ethiopia's Health Extension Program (HEP), delivered by over 38,000 female Health Extension Workers (HEWs), has been central to reducing preventable childhood deaths through community-based vaccination, malaria prevention, and treatment services.

### Overview
Childhood illness in Ethiopia is shaped by the intersection of infectious disease, malnutrition, incomplete vaccination coverage, indoor air pollution from biomass cooking fires, and limited access to quality healthcare — particularly in rural areas more than 10 kilometers from the nearest health facility. The following conditions represent the leading causes of childhood morbidity and mortality in Ethiopia.

### Clinical Profiles

* **Pneumonia** (*Amharic:* ሳምባ ነቀርሳ, *Samba Nekersa* | *Afaan Oromoo:* *Dhukkuba Somba*): The single leading infectious cause of death in Ethiopian children under five, accounting for approximately 18% of all under-five deaths. Pneumonia is primarily caused by *Streptococcus pneumoniae* and *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib) in young Ethiopian children, though RSV and other viruses are also significant. Risk factors specific to Ethiopia include severe malnutrition, indoor cooking fires without adequate ventilation (present in the vast majority of rural households), incomplete vaccination, and HIV exposure. Symptoms include fast or labored breathing, chest wall indrawing, nasal flaring, fever, cough, and cyanosis (blue lips or fingernails) in severe cases. Severe pneumonia requires hospitalization; non-severe cases are treated with amoxicillin, available free at health posts.

* **Diarrheal Disease** (*Amharic:* ተቅማጥ, *Teqimat* | *Afaan Oromoo:* *Garaakaasaa*): The second leading cause of child mortality in Ethiopia. Rotavirus is responsible for approximately 40% of severe diarrheal episodes in children under five. Other major pathogens include *Cryptosporidium*, *Shigella*, and enterotoxigenic *E. coli*. Dehydration is the primary cause of death. Treatment with Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — available at all health posts — and zinc supplementation for 10–14 days is the evidence-based standard of care. Continued feeding during illness is essential.

* **Measles** (*Amharic:* ኩፍኝ, *Kufign* | *Afaan Oromoo:* *Hanqaaquu*): Ethiopia has experienced repeated large-scale measles outbreaks due to gaps in vaccination coverage in remote regions and among pastoralist communities. Measles presents with high fever, cough, runny nose, red eyes (conjunctivitis), and the characteristic red rash beginning on the face and spreading downward. Complications — including pneumonia, brain inflammation (encephalitis), and blindness from Vitamin A deficiency — are responsible for measles deaths in Ethiopia. Vitamin A supplementation at time of measles diagnosis significantly reduces mortality.

* **Neonatal Conditions** (*Amharic:* የጨቅላ ሕፃናት ሕመም, *Yechiqqla Hitsanat Hemem*): Premature birth complications, neonatal sepsis (blood infection), and birth asphyxia together account for approximately 40% of all under-five deaths in Ethiopia. The majority of Ethiopian births occur at home without skilled attendance, significantly elevating neonatal mortality risk.

### Symptoms (General Warning Signs in Children)
* Fast or difficult breathing (more than 50 breaths per minute in infants)
* Chest indrawing, nasal flaring, or grunting
* High fever (above 38.5°C) or abnormally low temperature
* Inability to breastfeed or drink
* Persistent vomiting
* Convulsions or loss of consciousness
* Extreme lethargy or unusual drowsiness


## 8. Trachoma
**Amharic:** ትራኮማ (*Trachoma*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Akka Ijaa* (literally "eye disease")

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia has the highest burden of blinding trachoma in the world, accounting for approximately 40% of global trachoma-associated blindness cases.
* An estimated 76 million Ethiopians live in trachoma-endemic areas, with the Amhara region having the world's single highest district-level prevalence — over 50% active trachoma in children aged 1–9 years in some communities.
* Trachoma is the world's leading infectious cause of preventable blindness, caused by the bacterium *Chlamydia trachomatis*.
* The disease predominantly affects women (who are twice as likely as men to develop blinding trichiasis due to their close contact with infected children) and children under ten years old.

### Overview
Trachoma is a chronic keratoconjunctivitis (inflammation of the eye surface) caused by repeated infections with *Chlamydia trachomatis*. A single infection typically resolves without causing permanent damage; it is the scarring caused by repeated re-infections over years that progressively leads to blindness.

The disease cycle follows a devastating trajectory: active infection in young children causes follicular conjunctivitis (inflammation of the inner eyelid); with repeated re-infection, the inner eyelid becomes scarred and contracts, causing the eyelashes to turn inward — a condition called trichiasis. With every blink, inturned lashes scratch the cornea, causing intense, unrelenting pain and progressive corneal scarring that eventually leads to irreversible blindness.

Ethiopia's trachoma crisis is concentrated in the Amhara region (where prevalence in children historically reached 65% in some communities), Oromia, the Southern Nations, Nationalities and Peoples' Region (SNNPR), Benishangul-Gumuz, and Tigray. The disease spreads when ocular or nasal discharge from infected individuals — particularly young children who serve as the primary reservoirs — is transmitted via direct contact (touching infected eyes or faces) or by the *Musca sorbens* fly, which is attracted to facial secretions. Shared towels, cloths, and poor facial hygiene in water-scarce communities facilitate transmission.

The Ethiopian government, in partnership with international NGOs, implements the WHO-endorsed **SAFE strategy** — Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, and Environmental improvement — as the national framework for trachoma elimination.

### Causes of Trachoma
Trachoma is caused by *Chlamydia trachomatis* serovars A, B, Ba, and C. Transmission routes in Ethiopia include:
* Direct contact with ocular or nasal discharge from infected individuals (predominantly children)
* *Musca sorbens* fly transmission between faces
* Shared facial cloths or towels in water-scarce households
* Inadequate facial cleanliness, particularly among children, in communities with limited water access

### Symptoms
**Active Trachoma (in children):**
* Itching and irritation of the eyes
* Mucoid or purulent (pus-containing) discharge from the eyes
* Follicles (small bumps) on the inner surface of the upper eyelid
* Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
* Watery eyes

**Advanced Trachoma — Trichiasis (in adults):**
* Eyelashes turned inward, visibly scratching the cornea
* Constant, severe eye pain with every blink
* Progressive corneal opacity (cloudiness) and visual impairment
* Irreversible blindness in untreated advanced cases


## 9. Dengue Fever
**Amharic:** ዴንጊ ወባ (*Dengi Weba*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Dhukkuba Denguu*

### Key Facts
* Dengue is an emerging and rapidly expanding public health concern in Ethiopia, with confirmed outbreaks increasingly reported in urban and peri-urban lowland areas.
* Unlike malaria, which is transmitted by the night-biting *Anopheles* mosquito, dengue is transmitted by *Aedes aegypti*, a day-biting mosquito that thrives in urban environments with stagnant water in containers, tires, and drainage systems.
* Ethiopia's first large confirmed dengue outbreaks were documented in Dire Dawa city and parts of the Afar region, with subsequent outbreaks reported in Logia (Afar), Semera (Afar), and increasingly in Oromia lowland towns.
* There is currently no specific antiviral treatment for dengue; management is supportive.

### Overview
Dengue fever is caused by any of four closely related dengue virus serotypes (DENV-1 through DENV-4), transmitted primarily by the *Aedes aegypti* mosquito. Unlike most African mosquito-borne diseases, *Aedes aegypti* is a highly domesticated, urban-adapted mosquito that breeds in small collections of clean or slightly contaminated stagnant water — flower pots, water storage containers, discarded tires, blocked gutters, and construction site water pools — all of which are abundant in Ethiopia's rapidly growing secondary cities.

A critical feature of dengue epidemiology with particular relevance to Ethiopia is the risk of severe disease upon re-infection with a different serotype. A person who recovers from infection with one dengue serotype is immune to that serotype but remains susceptible to the other three; upon subsequent infection with a different serotype, immunological cross-reactivity can paradoxically amplify the infection, greatly increasing the risk of the life-threatening severe dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome).

As Ethiopia's urban population grows — with cities like Dire Dawa, Adama, Hawassa, Jimma, and Bahir Dar expanding rapidly — and as climate change extends the geographic range of *Aedes aegypti* into historically cooler highland areas, the dengue burden in Ethiopia is expected to increase substantially.

### Causes of Dengue Fever
Dengue is caused by a *Flavivirus* transmitted through the bite of an infected *Aedes aegypti* mosquito, and to a lesser extent *Aedes albopictus*. These mosquitoes acquire the virus by feeding on an infected human host and transmit it to the next person they bite after an incubation period of 8–12 days within the mosquito. The virus incubates in the human host for 4–10 days before symptoms appear.

### Symptoms

**Dengue Fever (Classic):**
* Sudden onset of high fever (39–40°C)
* Severe headache, particularly behind the eyes (retro-orbital pain)
* Severe joint and muscle pain — historically termed "breakbone fever" due to its intensity
* Skin rash appearing 2–5 days after fever onset, beginning on the trunk
* Nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite
* Mild bleeding manifestations: easy bruising, nosebleeds, bleeding gums

**Severe Dengue — Warning Signs (Medical Emergency):**
* Severe abdominal pain or persistent vomiting
* Rapid breathing and clinical signs of circulatory failure
* Bleeding from the gums, nose, or in vomit or stools
* Fatigue, restlessness, and extreme weakness
* Sudden drop in fever accompanied by deterioration in clinical condition
* Plasma leakage (fluid accumulating in body cavities, particularly the chest and abdomen)


## 10. HIV/AIDS

**Amharic:** ኤች አይ ቪ / ኤድስ (*HIV/AIDS*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *HIV/AIDS*

### Key Facts
* Ethiopia has made significant progress in reducing HIV infections and AIDS-related deaths over the past two decades.
* Approximately 600,000 Ethiopians are living with HIV.
* Urban areas generally have higher HIV prevalence than rural areas.
* HIV weakens the immune system and increases the risk of tuberculosis and other infections.

### Causes
HIV is transmitted through:
* Unprotected sexual contact
* Sharing contaminated needles
* Mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, birth, or breastfeeding
* Transfusion of infected blood

### Symptoms
Early symptoms may include:
* Fever
* Sore throat
* Rash
* Swollen lymph nodes

Advanced symptoms may include:
* Weight loss
* Persistent diarrhea
* Recurrent infections
* Tuberculosis

### Prevention
* Consistent condom use
* HIV testing and counseling
* Antiretroviral therapy (ART)
* Prevention of mother-to-child transmission programs


## 11. Acute Watery Diarrhea (AWD)

**Amharic:** ተቅማጥ (*Teqimat*) | **Afaan Oromoo:** *Garaa Kaasaa*

### Key Facts
* Acute watery diarrhea outbreaks are common in Ethiopia.
* AWD often occurs during droughts, floods, and humanitarian emergencies.
* Children under five are most vulnerable.

### Symptoms
* Frequent watery stools
* Vomiting
* Thirst
* Dehydration
* Weakness

### Prevention
* Handwashing with soap
* Safe drinking water
* Proper sanitation
* Early use of Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

### When to Seek Care
Seek immediate medical attention if:
* There is blood in the stool
* The child cannot drink
* Severe dehydration develops
* Vomiting prevents fluid intake

## 12. Maternal Health

**Amharic:** የእናቶች ጤና (*Yenatoch Tena*)  
**Afaan Oromoo:** *Fayyaa Haadholii*

### Key Facts
* Maternal mortality has declined significantly in Ethiopia but remains a public health concern.
* Major causes include:
  * Severe bleeding after childbirth
  * Infection
  * High blood pressure during pregnancy
  * Obstructed labor

### Warning Signs During Pregnancy
* Severe headache
* Swelling of face and hands
* Vaginal bleeding
* Severe abdominal pain
* Reduced fetal movement

### Prevention
* Attend antenatal care visits
* Deliver at a health facility
* Seek emergency care for danger signs


## 13. Public Health Infrastructure and Community Resources in Ethiopia

### Ethiopia's Health Extension Program (HEP)
Ethiopia's Health Extension Program is one of the largest community health worker programs in Africa. Over 38,000 female Health Extension Workers (HEWs) — *Amharic:* ሄልዝ ኤክስቴንሽን ወርከሮች — are deployed at the kebele (village) level, each serving approximately 500 households. HEWs provide:
* Malaria rapid diagnostic testing and treatment
* Vitamin A supplementation and growth monitoring
* Vaccination and routine immunization
* TB sputum sample collection and DOTS support
* Family planning counseling and services
* Health education on water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)
* Referral to higher-level health facilities

### National Vaccination Schedule (Ethiopia EPI)
All vaccines in Ethiopia's national immunization program are provided free of charge:
* **BCG (TB):** At birth
* **Hepatitis B:** At birth
* **OPV/IPV (Polio):** Multiple doses
* **Pentavalent (DPT-HepB-Hib):** 6, 10, and 14 weeks
* **Pneumococcal (PCV10):** 6, 10, and 14 weeks
* **Rotavirus:** 6 and 10 weeks
* **Measles-Rubella:** 9 months and 18 months
* **Meningococcal A (MenAfriVac):** Single dose at 9–18 months in high-risk regions

# Ethiopia Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

---

## Condition: Malaria

**Profile:**
Malaria is the leading cause of outpatient visits and one of the top causes of death in Ethiopia, with an estimated 3.8 million clinical cases annually. Unlike most of Sub-Saharan Africa, Ethiopia has a mixed burden: approximately 60% P. falciparum and 40% P. vivax — the highest proportion of P. vivax in the region. P. vivax can form dormant liver stages (hypnozoites) causing relapses months to years after the initial infection. Malaria is endemic in approximately 75% of Ethiopian territory, putting an estimated 60 million people at risk.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Malaria, malaria fever
- Amharic: ወባ (Weba)
- Oromo: Busaa

**Symptoms:**
- Cyclic attacks of shivering and chills, followed by high fever (38.5°C or above) and sweating
- Severe headache, muscle pain, joint aches, and generalized fatigue
- Nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite
- Severe anemia (pallor, weakness, rapid heartbeat)
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes)
- In severe P. falciparum cases: convulsions, altered consciousness, respiratory distress, and organ failure

**Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Hospital Care:**
- Convulsions or loss of consciousness
- Inability to stand or sit up
- Repeated vomiting
- Dark or cola-colored urine
- Severe pallor or yellow eyes

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH Ethiopia):**
- Every suspected case must be confirmed by RDT or microscopy before treatment
- P. falciparum first-line treatment: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)
- P. vivax treatment: Chloroquine plus Primaquine (Primaquine kills hypnozoites to prevent relapse — check for G6PD deficiency before prescribing)
- Severe malaria: intravenous Artesunate at hospital
- Prevention: LLINs; indoor residual spraying; IPTp-SP for pregnant women; malaria education by Health Extension Workers (HEWs)

## Condition: Typhoid Fever

**Profile:**
Typhoid fever is endemic in Ethiopia, particularly in urban and peri-urban areas of Addis Ababa, Dire Dawa, Hawassa, and Bahir Dar where water supply is unreliable and street food consumption is common. School children and young adults aged 5–25 are disproportionately affected. Drug-resistant typhoid (MDR Salmonella Typhi) is an increasing concern.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Typhoid, typhoid fever
- Amharic: ታይፎይድ ወባ (Tayfoid Weba)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba Taayifoyidii

**Symptoms:**
- Gradually rising step-wise fever reaching 39–40°C by end of first week
- Severe persistent headache and general malaise
- Abdominal pain, bloating, constipation (adults) or diarrhea (children)
- Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
- Rose spots — faint salmon-colored rash on the trunk in second week
- Enlarged liver and spleen
- Confusion and altered mental status in severe untreated cases

**Complications Requiring Emergency Care:**
Intestinal perforation and internal bleeding are life-threatening. Any sudden severe abdominal pain or rapid deterioration requires immediate hospital care.

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH):**
- Diagnosis: blood culture (gold standard) or Widal test
- First-line: Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin
- MDR cases: Ceftriaxone IV
- Complete the full antibiotic course
- Prevention: boil or treat all drinking water; wash hands before eating and after using the toilet; avoid raw vegetables irrigated with wastewater

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

**Profile:**
Ethiopia ranks among the 30 high-TB burden countries globally. Estimated TB incidence is approximately 140 cases per 100,000 population annually — roughly 165,000 new cases per year. HIV co-infection is present in approximately 10–12% of TB patients. Drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) is an increasing concern. TB services are free at all public health facilities through the DOTS strategy, delivered at the community level by over 38,000 Health Extension Workers (HEWs).

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: TB, tuberculosis, chest disease
- Amharic: ነቀርሳ (Nekersa)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba Sombaa (lung disease)

**Symptoms:**
- Persistent cough lasting more than two weeks — may produce blood-stained sputum
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
- Extreme fatigue and general weakness
- In extrapulmonary TB: swollen lymph nodes, back pain (spinal TB), or neurological symptoms (TB meningitis)

**Ethiopia-Specific Context:**
A small proportion of TB cases — particularly in rural communities — are caused by Mycobacterium bovis transmitted through consumption of unpasteurized milk and raw meat (e.g., kitfo, raw minced beef). Pasteurize or boil milk; cook meat thoroughly.

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH — DOTS Strategy):**
- Diagnosis: GeneXpert (preferred) or sputum microscopy
- Treatment: 6-month course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol — free at all public facilities
- Never stop treatment early — creates MDR-TB
- All TB patients tested for HIV; all HIV patients screened for TB at every visit
- Community-level delivery by Health Extension Workers (HEWs)

## Condition: Cholera

**Profile:**
Ethiopia experiences recurrent cholera outbreaks, particularly in drought-affected regions, flood-prone lowlands, and urban informal settlements. The Afar, Somali, and Oromia regions bear the greatest burden, with outbreaks frequently crossing borders from neighboring Somalia, South Sudan, and Kenya. The 2022–2023 outbreak was one of the largest in recent history, affecting multiple regions simultaneously.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Cholera, acute watery diarrhea
- Amharic: ኮሌራ (Kolera)
- Oromo: Koleeraa

**Symptoms:**
- Acute sudden-onset profuse watery diarrhea — rice-water appearance with fishy odor
- Projectile, effortless vomiting
- Severe rapid dehydration: sunken eyes, dry mouth, decreased skin elasticity, no urine output
- Muscle cramps, particularly in the legs
- Rapid weak pulse and low blood pressure in severe cases

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Begin ORS immediately and go to a health facility. Do not wait. Fatal dehydration can occur within hours.

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH):**
- Begin ORS immediately at home
- Severe dehydration: intravenous Ringer's Lactate
- Children: Zinc supplementation for 10–14 days
- Antibiotics reserved for severe cases
- Prevention: boil or treat all drinking water; wash hands with soap; avoid consuming food from contaminated water sources; safe handling of deceased persons during funeral practices

## Condition: Malnutrition

**Profile:**
Ethiopia has one of the highest rates of childhood malnutrition in the world. Approximately 37% of children under five are stunted — among the ten highest rates globally. Acute malnutrition affects approximately 9% of children nationally, rising above 20% in drought-affected areas of the Somali, Afar, and parts of Oromia regions. Malnutrition is an underlying factor in approximately 45% of all child deaths in Ethiopia. The 2020–2022 Tigray conflict and recurring droughts have created acute nutritional emergencies affecting millions.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Malnutrition, wasting, stunting, kwashiorkor
- Amharic: ምግብ እጥት (Migib Ithot — food deficiency)
- Oromo: Gad-dhabbuu

**Symptoms:**
- In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones
- MUAC below 11.5 cm = severe acute malnutrition
- Bilateral pitting edema (swollen face, feet, legs) — kwashiorkor
- Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
- Extreme lethargy, irritability, and loss of interest in surroundings
- Persistent infections and delayed wound healing
- Night blindness (Vitamin A deficiency)

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH/UNICEF):**
- SAM: RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food) at Therapeutic Feeding Centers (TFCs) or Community-based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) sites
- Do not give regular food to a severely malnourished child without medical guidance
- Prevention: exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; appropriate complementary feeding; Vitamin A supplementation; deworming; Ethiopia's Productive Safety Net Programme (PSNP) for food-insecure households

## Condition: Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

**Profile:**
Hypertension is the most common non-communicable disease in Ethiopia and is largely undiagnosed — over 60% of affected adults are unaware of their condition. Population studies estimate prevalence at 20–28% among Ethiopian adults, with higher rates in urban areas (approximately 31%). It is a leading risk factor for stroke, heart attack, kidney failure, and heart failure. Khat (Catha edulis) chewing — widespread in Oromia, Harari, and Dire Dawa — is associated with acute and chronic blood pressure elevation.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Hypertension, high blood pressure
- Amharic: የደም ጊፊት (Yedem Gifit — literally "blood pressure")
- Oromo: Dhiibbaa Dhiigaa Olka'aa

**Symptoms:**
Most people have no symptoms. When they occur:
- Persistent headache at the back of the head — typically worse in the morning
- Dizziness, lightheadedness, or episodes of blurred vision
- Nosebleeds in severe cases
- Shortness of breath and chest discomfort
- Palpitations

**Hypertensive Crisis** (BP above 180/120 mmHg): severe headache, confusion, visual disturbances, chest pain — go to hospital immediately.

**Ethiopia-Specific Risk Factors:**
- High salt intake through injera condiments and processed foods
- Khat (Catha edulis) chewing — associated with elevated blood pressure and cardiac arrhythmias
- Chronic psychosocial stress related to poverty, displacement, and conflict
- Physical inactivity from increasingly sedentary urban lifestyles
- Traditional alcoholic beverages: tella, tej, areke

**Management:**
- Reduce salt intake; eat more fruits and vegetables
- Stop khat chewing and limit alcohol
- Exercise regularly
- Take prescribed medication daily without stopping — even when feeling well
- Regular blood pressure monitoring at health centers (free at public facilities)

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health

**Profile:**
Ethiopia has made remarkable progress in reducing maternal mortality, but significant challenges remain. The leading causes of maternal death are postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia, obstructed labor, and sepsis. The Health Extension Program (HEP) has been central to improving ANC coverage and supervised delivery rates. The FMoH mandates a minimum of 8 ANC visits and skilled attendance at all deliveries.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Pregnancy complications, postpartum bleeding, eclampsia
- Amharic: የወሊድ ችግሮች (Yewelid Chigiroch — delivery problems)
- Oromo: Rakkoo ulfaa (pregnancy problems)

**Danger Signs During Pregnancy:**
- Severe persistent headache or blurred vision — signs of pre-eclampsia
- Swollen face, hands, or legs
- Any vaginal bleeding
- Reduced or absent fetal movement
- Fever with lower abdominal pain

**Danger Signs After Delivery:**
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (postpartum hemorrhage)
- High fever with foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Convulsions (eclampsia)

**Danger Signs in Newborns:**
- Fast or difficult breathing
- Inability to breastfeed or swallow
- Cold skin or high fever
- Yellowing of skin or eyes within 24 hours
- Continuous high-pitched crying

**National Care Guidelines (FMoH):**
- Minimum 8 ANC contacts at a health center
- Oxytocin (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately after delivery
- Chlorhexidine gel on umbilical cord stump daily
- All deliveries under skilled birth attendance at a health facility
- Newborn: Vitamin K, eye drops, BCG and Hepatitis B vaccines at birth
- Community-level support through Health Extension Workers (HEWs) and Women's Development Armies

## Condition: HIV and AIDS

**Profile:**
Ethiopia has approximately 730,000 people living with HIV. HIV/TB co-infection is a major driver of TB deaths. The FMoH provides free ART at all public health facilities. Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission (PMTCT) services are integrated into all ANC clinics.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: HIV, AIDS
- Amharic: ኤችአይቪ (HIV), ኤድስ (AIDS)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba AIDS

**Symptoms:**
- Early: flu-like illness 2–4 weeks after exposure — fever, rash, swollen lymph nodes
- Latent stage: asymptomatic for years
- Advanced AIDS: rapid weight loss, chronic diarrhea, oral thrush, recurring infections, night sweats

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH):**
- HIV testing free and confidential at all public health facilities
- ART free and initiated as soon as possible after diagnosis
- Preferred first-line regimen: Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir (TLD)
- All HIV patients screened for TB at every visit
- PMTCT: all pregnant women tested; HIV-positive mothers on ART throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding

## Condition: Dengue Fever

**Profile:**
Dengue fever is an emerging concern in Ethiopia, documented in Dire Dawa, Djibouti border areas, and parts of Afar and Somali regions. Climate change is expanding the range of the Aedes aegypti mosquito — which bites during the day — further into Ethiopia.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Dengue, dengue fever
- Amharic: የዴንጊ ወባ (Ye-Dengi Weba)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba Dengii

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C) lasting 2–7 days
- Severe headache and pain behind the eyes
- Intense muscle and joint pain
- Nausea, vomiting, and swollen glands
- Rash 3–4 days after fever onset
- Severe dengue: bleeding gums, blood in vomit or stool — medical emergency

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care: rest, fluids, paracetamol
- Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen
- Severe dengue requires hospital admission

## Condition: Scabies

**Profile:**
Scabies is common in Ethiopia, particularly in overcrowded urban slums, internally displaced persons (IDP) camps, and rural communities with limited access to clean water. Mass outbreaks have occurred in displacement settings.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Scabies
- Amharic: የቆዳ ማሳከክ (Yekoda Masakek — skin itching)
- Oromo: Qoraan gogaa (skin itch)

**Symptoms:**
- Intense itching, especially at night
- Pimple-like rash with small red bumps and blisters
- Burrow tracks between fingers, wrists, elbows, armpits, and waistline

**Treatment:**
- Permethrin 5% cream to entire body from neck to toes; leave 8–12 hours; wash off
- All household members treated simultaneously
- Wash all bedding and clothing in hot water
- Repeat after one week
- In settings without Permethrin: benzyl benzoate lotion is an available alternative

## Condition: Ringworm (Tinea)

**Profile:**
Ringworm is a common fungal skin infection in Ethiopia, particularly among school-age children. Scalp ringworm (tinea capitis) is especially prevalent in schools and childcare settings.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Ringworm, fungal infection
- Amharic: የቁስለት ፈንጋይ (Yekuslat Fengay — fungal wound)
- Oromo: Buusaa gogaa (skin fungus)

**Symptoms:**
- Circular, scaly, ring-shaped patches with raised edges (body)
- Scaly scalp patches with hair loss — very common in Ethiopian school children
- Scaling and cracking between toes (athlete's foot)
- Red itchy rash in groin (jock itch)

**Treatment:**
- Body and foot: topical antifungal cream (clotrimazole) for 2–4 weeks
- Scalp: oral griseofulvin — topical creams do not penetrate the scalp
- Keep areas dry; do not share combs, towels, hats, or pillows

## Condition: Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

**Profile:**
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It is not contagious. On darker skin tones — as is typical in Ethiopia — eczema often appears as dark brown, grey, or purple patches rather than the red appearance common in medical textbooks, leading to frequent misdiagnosis.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Eczema, atopic dermatitis
- Amharic: የቆዳ ደረቅነት (Yekoda Dereknet — dry skin condition)
- Oromo: Gogaa goguu (dry skin)

**Symptoms:**
- Dry, sensitive skin with intense itching — worse at night
- Dark brown, grey, or purple patches on darker skin
- Rough, leathery, or scaly patches
- Common locations: inside elbows, behind knees, hands, feet, face, neck

**Management:**
- Moisturize daily with fragrance-free emollients — shea butter and locally available plant oils are effective
- Mild unscented soap; avoid hot water baths
- Hydrocortisone cream (1%) for flare-ups
- Avoid triggers: harsh soaps, dust, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Seek care if skin becomes warm, weeping, or crusted

## Condition: Conjunctivitis and Trachoma

**Profile:**
Ethiopia has one of the highest burdens of trachoma globally — a chronic bacterial eye infection caused by Chlamydia trachomatis that is the world's leading infectious cause of preventable blindness. Active trachoma affects millions of Ethiopian children, particularly in rural areas with limited access to clean water and sanitation. Acute conjunctivitis (bacterial and viral) is also extremely common.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Pink eye, conjunctivitis, trachoma
- Amharic: የዓይን ህመም (Ye'ayin Himmem — eye pain), ትራኮማ (Trachoma)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba ija (eye disease)

**Symptoms (Acute Conjunctivitis):**
- Red or pink whites of the eye
- Thick yellow or green discharge crusting eyelashes on waking
- Increased tearing, burning, and gritty sensation
- Sensitivity to light

**Symptoms (Trachoma):**
- Repeated episodes of eye discharge and redness in children
- Eyelid scarring from repeated infections
- Eyelashes turning inward and scratching the cornea (trichiasis) — causing severe pain and progressive blindness
- Most common in communities without access to clean water for face washing

**Treatment:**
- Acute bacterial conjunctivitis: tetracycline or chloramphenicol eye ointment
- Trachoma: azithromycin (single oral dose) — distributed through Mass Drug Administration (MDA) campaigns
- Trachoma prevention: the SAFE strategy (Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement)
- Surgical correction (trichiasis surgery) for advanced trachoma preventing blindness

## Condition: Jaundice

**Profile:**
Jaundice in Ethiopia is most commonly caused by malaria, viral hepatitis (Hepatitis B and E), sickle cell disease, and liver disease. Neonatal jaundice is a significant cause of preventable newborn brain damage and death.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Jaundice, yellow eyes
- Amharic: ቢጫ ዓይን (Bicha Ayin — yellow eyes)
- Oromo: Ija biiftuu (yellow eyes)

**Symptoms:**
- Yellow discoloration of skin and whites of the eyes
- Dark, tea-colored urine
- Pale or clay-colored stools
- Itching, fatigue, and abdominal pain

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
- Jaundice in a newborn within 24 hours of birth — requires urgent phototherapy
- Jaundice with fever, severe abdominal pain, or confusion — medical emergency
- Any jaundice lasting more than 2 weeks should be evaluated

## Condition: Monkeypox (Mpox)

**Profile:**
Ethiopia has documented mpox cases and is at risk given its proximity to high-burden DRC and Sudan. Surveillance is active through the Ethiopian Public Health Institute (EPHI) in collaboration with WHO.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Monkeypox, mpox
- Amharic: የዝንጀሮ ፈንጣጣ (Ye-Zinjero Fentata — monkey smallpox)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba mpox

**Symptoms:**
- Fever, severe headache, swollen lymph nodes, back pain, muscle aches
- Rash 1–3 days after fever — begins on face, spreads to palms, soles, and body
- Rash progresses: flat spots → raised bumps → blisters → pustules → scabs

**When to Seek Care:**
Unexplained rash with fever and swollen lymph nodes — seek care, avoid close contact. Report to EPHI.

## Condition: Sickle Cell Disease

**Profile:**
Sickle cell disease is present in Ethiopia though at lower prevalence than West Africa. It is more common in certain ethnic communities and in malaria-endemic lowland regions where the sickle cell trait historically provided partial malaria protection.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Sickle cell, sickle cell anaemia
- Amharic: የደም ሕዋስ ሕመም (Yedem Hiwas Himmem — blood cell disease)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba dhiigaa

**Symptoms:**
- Episodes of severe pain in chest, abdomen, joints, and bones
- Chronic anemia: fatigue, pallor, yellowish eyes
- Swollen hands and feet in infants
- Frequent infections
- Delayed growth
- Stroke risk in young children

**Management:**
- Daily penicillin for children under 5
- Daily folic acid
- Hydroxyurea to reduce crisis frequency
- Adequate hydration
- Immediate care for painful crisis, chest pain, or fever
- Genetic counseling available at major hospitals in Addis Ababa

## Condition: Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia)

**Profile:**
Schistosomiasis is endemic in Ethiopia, particularly in communities near Lake Tana (Amhara region), the Rift Valley lakes, the Awash River basin, and irrigation schemes. Communities that fish, bathe, or farm in freshwater bodies are at highest risk.

**How Ethiopians Describe It:**
- English: Bilharzia, schistosomiasis
- Amharic: ቢልሃርዚያ (Bilharziya)
- Oromo: Dhukkuba bishaan (water disease)

**Symptoms:**
- Urogenital form: blood in urine, bladder pain, frequent urination
- Intestinal form: abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, enlarged liver and spleen
- Swimmer's itch shortly after freshwater exposure

**Treatment:**
- Praziquantel (single oral dose) — free during Mass Drug Administration campaigns
- Annual MDA in endemic communities
- Prevention: avoid swimming in Lake Tana, Rift Valley lakes, and irrigation canals; use treated water for bathing where possible

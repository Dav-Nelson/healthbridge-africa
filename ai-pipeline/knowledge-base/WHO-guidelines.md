# WHO Global Health Guidelines — Africa Baseline Reference

---

## SECTION 1: COMMUNICABLE (INFECTIOUS) DISEASES

---

### Condition: Malaria

**Profile:**
Malaria is a life-threatening illness caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted through the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. Five species infect humans: P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, and P. knowlesi. P. falciparum causes the most severe disease and accounts for the overwhelming majority of deaths in Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Global Burden:**
In 2023, Africa accounted for 94% of all malaria cases (263 million globally) and 95% of malaria deaths. Children under five represent approximately 76% of all malaria fatalities on the continent.

**Symptoms:**
- Cyclic attacks of shivering and chills, followed by high fever (38.5°C or above) and profuse sweating
- Severe headache, muscle pain, joint aches, and generalized fatigue
- Nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite
- In severe cases: severe anemia, jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes), convulsions, altered consciousness, respiratory distress, and organ failure
- In children: refusal to feed, lethargy, and fast breathing are danger signs

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Any child or adult with fever plus confusion, convulsions, difficulty breathing, inability to stand, or dark urine must be taken to a health facility immediately — these are signs of severe malaria.

**Prevention:**
- Sleep under a Long-Lasting Insecticidal Net (LLIN) every night
- Use indoor residual spraying where available
- Pregnant women should receive Intermittent Preventive Treatment (IPTp)
- The RTS,S/AS01 malaria vaccine is now available in parts of Africa for children

### Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

**Profile:**
Tuberculosis is an airborne infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It primarily affects the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can spread through the bloodstream to affect the spine, abdomen, brain, kidneys, and skin (extrapulmonary TB). It spreads when a person with active TB coughs, sneezes, sings, or spits.

**Global Burden:**
Africa accounts for 25% of new global TB infections and approximately 42% of global TB deaths. TB is the leading cause of death among people living with HIV.

**Symptoms:**
- Persistent cough lasting more than two weeks, which may produce blood-stained sputum
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
- Extreme fatigue and general weakness
- Swollen lymph nodes, back pain, or neurological symptoms in extrapulmonary TB

**When to Seek Care:**
Any cough lasting more than two weeks — especially with weight loss, night sweats, or blood in sputum — must be evaluated at a health facility. TB is curable when caught early.

**Treatment:**
Standard first-line treatment is a 6-month course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol. Treatment is free at public health facilities across Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia, and Kenya. Stopping treatment early creates drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB), which is far harder to treat.

### Condition: HIV and AIDS

**Profile:**
HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) attacks and destroys CD4 T-lymphocytes, progressively breaking down the immune system. AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the advanced stage characterized by severe immune failure and opportunistic infections. Sub-Saharan Africa carries nearly two-thirds of all new global HIV infections.

**Symptoms by Stage:**
- Stage 1 (Acute, 2–4 weeks post-exposure): Flu-like illness — fever, swollen lymph nodes, rash on the torso, sore throat, mouth ulcers
- Stage 2 (Latency, up to 10 years): Often asymptomatic; mild persistent swollen lymph nodes
- Stage 3 (AIDS): Rapid unexplained weight loss, chronic diarrhea lasting more than a week, oral thrush (white coating on tongue), severe shortness of breath, recurring infections, and night sweats

**When to Seek Care:**
Anyone who has had unprotected sex, shared needles, or received unscreened blood should get tested. HIV testing is free and confidential at health centers. With Antiretroviral Therapy (ART), people with HIV can live long, healthy lives.

### Condition: Cholera

**Profile:**
Cholera is a hyper-acute bacterial intestinal infection caused by Vibrio cholerae, transmitted through food or water contaminated with infected feces. It can cause fatal dehydration within hours if untreated. Outbreaks are closely linked to floods, displacement, and inadequate sanitation.

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden onset of painless, profuse watery diarrhea with a characteristic "rice-water" appearance
- Projectile vomiting
- Rapid, severe dehydration: sunken eyes, dry mouth, loss of skin elasticity, absence of urination, muscle cramps
- In severe untreated cases: low blood pressure, weak rapid pulse, and hypovolemic shock

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Any person — especially a child — with profuse watery diarrhea and vomiting must receive Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) immediately and be taken to a health facility without delay.

**Treatment:**
- Begin ORS immediately — do not wait to travel
- Severe cases require intravenous Ringer's Lactate
- Children require Zinc supplementation for 10–14 days
- Antibiotics (Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin) are reserved for severe cases only

### Condition: Typhoid Fever

**Profile:**
Typhoid fever is a systemic bacterial infection caused by Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi, transmitted through the fecal-oral route via food or water contaminated with the feces of an infected person. It is endemic across Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia, and Kenya, particularly in areas with unreliable water supply and poor sanitation.

**Symptoms:**
- Gradually rising fever in a step-wise pattern, reaching 39–40°C by the end of the first week
- Severe persistent headache and general malaise
- Abdominal pain, bloating, and either constipation (adults) or diarrhea (children)
- Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
- Rose spots: faint salmon-colored rash on the trunk in the second week
- Enlarged liver and spleen
- Confusion and altered mental status in severe untreated cases

**Complications (requiring emergency care):**
Intestinal bleeding, intestinal perforation, and blood poisoning (septicemia) can occur in untreated or undertreated cases. These are life-threatening emergencies.

**Treatment:**
Antibiotics including Azithromycin, Ciprofloxacin, or Ceftriaxone depending on local resistance patterns. Drug-resistant typhoid is a growing concern — always complete the full course.

---

### Condition: Malaria vs. Typhoid — How to Tell the Difference

Both cause fever and are common across Africa. Key differences:
- Malaria fever is typically cyclic (comes and goes in waves); typhoid fever rises gradually and stays high
- Malaria often causes severe muscle and joint pain; typhoid more commonly causes abdominal pain and bloating
- A Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) confirms malaria; Widal test or blood culture confirms typhoid
- Self-treating for one when you have the other is dangerous — always test before treating

### Condition: Measles

**Profile:**
Measles is a highly contagious viral respiratory infection caused by the measles virus (Morbillivirus). It spreads through airborne droplets and direct contact with nasal or throat secretions. It is entirely vaccine-preventable but remains a leading cause of childhood death in Africa due to incomplete vaccination coverage.

**Symptoms:**
- High fever (often above 40°C)
- Cough, runny nose, and red, watery eyes (conjunctivitis) — appearing 2–4 days before the rash
- Koplik's spots: tiny white spots inside the mouth — a distinctive early sign
- Red, blotchy rash starting on the face and spreading downward to the neck, trunk, arms, and legs
- Rash typically appears 3–5 days after the first symptoms

**Complications:**
Pneumonia (the leading cause of measles death), encephalitis (brain inflammation), severe diarrhea, ear infections, and blindness — especially in malnourished children with Vitamin A deficiency.

**When to Seek Care:**
Any child with a fever and spreading rash should be seen at a health facility. Measles is notifiable — health workers must report every case.

**Prevention:**
Two doses of the measles-containing vaccine (MCV1 at 9 months, MCV2 at 15 months) provide lifelong protection.

### Condition: Chickenpox (Varicella)

**Profile:**
Chickenpox is a highly contagious viral infection caused by the Varicella-zoster virus. It spreads through airborne droplets and direct contact with fluid from blisters. Most cases occur in children but can be severe in adults, newborns, and immunocompromised individuals.

**Symptoms:**
- Itchy, blister-like rash that begins on the chest, back, and face, then spreads across the body
- Rash progresses through three stages: red spots → fluid-filled blisters → crusted scabs
- Fever, fatigue, loss of appetite, and headache appearing 1–2 days before the rash
- All three stages of the rash may be present at the same time on the same person

**Complications:**
Bacterial skin infections from scratching, pneumonia, encephalitis, and in pregnant women — severe risk to the unborn baby.

**When to Seek Care:**
Seek care immediately if the rash spreads to the eyes, the person has difficulty breathing, or the rash becomes very red, warm, or tender — signs of bacterial superinfection.

### Condition: Monkeypox (Mpox)

**Profile:**
Mpox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the Monkeypox virus (MPXV), endemic to Central and West Africa. It spreads through close physical contact with an infected person's rash, scabs, or body fluids, and through respiratory droplets during prolonged face-to-face contact. Nigeria, Ghana, and the DRC are among the most affected countries.

**Symptoms:**
- Fever, severe headache, swollen lymph nodes (a distinguishing feature from smallpox), back pain, and muscle aches — appearing 5–21 days after exposure
- A rash developing 1–3 days after fever onset, beginning on the face and spreading to the palms, soles, and body
- Rash progresses through stages: flat spots → raised bumps → blisters → pustules → scabs
- Lesions are typically deep-seated and well-defined, and may be painful or itchy

**When to Seek Care:**
Any person with an unexplained rash plus fever and swollen lymph nodes should seek care and avoid close contact with others to prevent spread.

### Condition: Meningitis

**Profile:**
Meningitis is inflammation of the membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord, most commonly caused by bacterial (Neisseria meningitidis, Streptococcus pneumoniae) or viral infections. Bacterial meningitis is life-threatening and requires emergency treatment. The "Meningitis Belt" stretching across Sub-Saharan Africa — including northern Nigeria, Ghana, and Ethiopia — experiences epidemic outbreaks, particularly in the dry season.

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden, severe headache — often described as the worst headache of the person's life
- High fever and neck stiffness (unable to touch chin to chest)
- Sensitivity to light (photophobia) and loud sounds
- Nausea, vomiting, and confusion
- In bacterial meningitis: a non-blanching purple or red rash (petechiae) — a medical emergency
- In infants: bulging fontanelle (soft spot on head), high-pitched cry, refusal to feed, and blank staring

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Meningitis is a medical emergency. Fever + stiff neck + severe headache = go to a hospital immediately. Every hour of delay increases the risk of death or permanent disability.

### Condition: Dengue Fever

**Profile:**
Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral illness transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, which bites during the day — unlike the Anopheles mosquito which transmits malaria and bites at night. Dengue is endemic in parts of Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia, and Kenya and is increasing in urban areas due to climate change and urbanization.

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C) lasting 2–7 days
- Severe headache, pain behind the eyes
- Severe muscle and joint pain — often called "breakbone fever"
- Nausea, vomiting, and swollen glands
- A rash appearing 3–4 days after the fever begins
- In severe dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever): bleeding from gums or nose, blood in vomit or stool, rapid breathing, and fatigue — a medical emergency

**How to Distinguish from Malaria:**
Both cause sudden high fever. Key difference: dengue causes severe pain behind the eyes and intense joint pain; the Aedes mosquito bites during the day. A blood test is needed to confirm.

**Treatment:**
No specific antiviral treatment exists. Management is supportive: rest, fluids (ORS or IV fluids), and paracetamol for fever. Aspirin and ibuprofen must be avoided — they increase bleeding risk.

### Condition: Scabies

**Profile:**
Scabies is a highly contagious skin infestation caused by the microscopic mite Sarcoptes scabiei, which burrows into the skin to lay eggs. It spreads through prolonged direct skin-to-skin contact and, less commonly, through shared bedding or clothing. It is common in overcrowded households and schools across Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Symptoms:**
- Intense itching, especially at night
- A pimple-like rash with small red bumps and blisters
- Thin, irregular burrow tracks in the skin — often visible between fingers, on wrists, elbows, armpits, waistline, and genitals
- In infants and young children: rash may also appear on the head, face, neck, palms, and soles

**Treatment:**
Permethrin 5% cream applied to the entire body from neck to toes and left on for 8–12 hours is the first-line treatment. All household members and close contacts must be treated simultaneously. Bedding and clothing should be washed in hot water.

### Condition: Ringworm (Tinea)

**Profile:**
Despite its name, ringworm is not caused by a worm — it is a fungal skin infection caused by dermatophyte fungi (Tinea species). It spreads through direct contact with infected people, animals, soil, or contaminated surfaces. It is common in children across Africa and thrives in warm, humid environments.

**Types and Symptoms:**
- Tinea corporis (body): circular, red, scaly, ring-shaped patches with a clear center and raised edges
- Tinea capitis (scalp): scaly patches on the scalp, hair loss, and broken hair shafts — common in children
- Tinea pedis (athlete's foot): scaling, itching, and cracking between the toes
- Tinea cruris (jock itch): red, itchy rash in the groin area

**Treatment:**
Topical antifungal creams (clotrimazole, miconazole) for body and foot ringworm. Scalp ringworm requires oral antifungal medication (griseofulvin or fluconazole) because topical treatments do not penetrate hair follicles.

### Condition: Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

**Profile:**
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing the skin to become dry, itchy, and inflamed. It is not contagious. It commonly begins in childhood and may persist into adulthood. The condition typically flares and then clears in cycles. On darker skin tones — common across Africa — eczema may appear as dark brown, purple, or grey patches rather than the red appearance described in many textbooks.

**Symptoms:**
- Dry, sensitive skin
- Intense itching, especially at night
- Dark brown, grey, or purple patches on darker skin (may appear red on lighter skin)
- Rough, leathery, or scaly skin patches
- Swollen skin from scratching
- Common locations: inside of elbows, behind knees, hands, feet, face, and neck

**Management:**
- Moisturize the skin daily with fragrance-free emollients
- Use mild, unscented soap and avoid hot water baths
- Topical corticosteroids (hydrocortisone cream) for flare-ups
- Avoid known triggers: harsh soaps, dust mites, certain fabrics, sweat, and stress
- Seek care if the skin becomes infected (warm, weeping, crusted)

### Condition: Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia)

**Profile:**
Schistosomiasis is a parasitic disease caused by blood flukes (Schistosoma mansoni and S. haematobium). Larvae released by freshwater snails penetrate human skin during contact with contaminated open water. Africa accounts for approximately 85% of global cases. It is particularly common in communities near rivers, lakes, and irrigation systems.

**Symptoms:**
- Intestinal schistosomiasis (S. mansoni): abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, enlarged liver and spleen
- Urogenital schistosomiasis (S. haematobium): blood in urine (hematuria), bladder damage, and in women — genital lesions that increase HIV transmission risk

**Treatment:**
Praziquantel (single oral dose) is the safe, effective, and inexpensive treatment. Mass Drug Administration (MDA) programs distribute it free in endemic areas.

### Condition: Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

**Profile:**
Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva — the thin membrane covering the white of the eye and the inner eyelids. It can be caused by viral or bacterial infection, allergies, or chemical irritants. Infectious conjunctivitis is highly contagious and spreads through direct contact with eye secretions or contaminated hands and surfaces.

**Symptoms:**
- Red or pink coloring in the white of the eye
- Increased tearing or watery discharge
- Thick yellow or green discharge that crusts over the eyelashes — especially on waking (bacterial)
- Itching or burning sensation in the eye
- Sensitivity to light
- Feeling of sand or grit in the eye

**Treatment:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis: antibiotic eye drops or ointment (chloramphenicol or tetracycline)
- Viral conjunctivitis: no antibiotic needed — resolves on its own in 1–2 weeks
- Clean discharge gently with a clean, damp cloth; do not share towels or pillows
- Wash hands frequently to prevent spread

### Condition: Jaundice

**Profile:**
Jaundice is not a disease itself — it is a symptom of an underlying condition causing a build-up of bilirubin in the blood. The skin and whites of the eyes turn yellow. In Africa, jaundice is most commonly caused by malaria, viral hepatitis (Hepatitis A, B, or E), sickle cell disease, or liver disease.

**Symptoms:**
- Yellow discoloration of the skin and whites of the eyes
- Dark, tea-colored urine
- Pale or clay-colored stools
- Itching of the skin
- Fatigue and abdominal pain
- Nausea and vomiting

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Jaundice in a newborn baby must be treated urgently — neonatal jaundice can cause brain damage if untreated. Jaundice in any person with fever, severe abdominal pain, or confusion is a medical emergency.

### Condition: Malnutrition

**Profile:**
Malnutrition encompasses both undernutrition (insufficient calories, protein, or micronutrients) and overnutrition (excess caloric intake). In Sub-Saharan Africa, undernutrition remains the dominant form and is an underlying factor in approximately 45% of all child deaths. It both causes and worsens infectious diseases, creating a deadly cycle.

**Clinical Forms:**
- Stunting (chronic malnutrition): low height-for-age from sustained nutritional deprivation — causes irreversible impairment to growth and brain development
- Wasting (acute malnutrition): low weight-for-height from recent, rapid weight loss — immediately life-threatening in severe form (SAM)
- Underweight: low weight-for-age — a combined indicator
- Micronutrient deficiencies: iron (anemia), Vitamin A (blindness, immune failure), iodine (goiter, intellectual impairment), zinc

**Symptoms:**
- In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones, reduced mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC below 11.5 cm = severe acute malnutrition)
- Bilateral pitting edema (swollen face, feet, and legs) — sign of kwashiorkor
- Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
- Extreme lethargy and irritability
- Persistent infections and slow wound healing
- Night blindness (Vitamin A deficiency)

**Treatment:**
Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) requires treatment with Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) and medical assessment at a health facility. Do not give a severely malnourished child regular food without medical guidance.

## SECTION 2: NON-COMMUNICABLE DISEASES (NCDs)

---

### Condition: Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

**Profile:**
Hypertension is defined as a sustained blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or above. It is the world's leading preventable cause of cardiovascular disease and stroke, and is widely called the "silent killer" because it causes no obvious symptoms for years while damaging the heart, kidneys, brain, and blood vessels. NCDs including hypertension now account for over one-fifth of all deaths in Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Symptoms:**
Most people have no symptoms. When symptoms do occur:
- Persistent headache, especially at the back of the head, typically worse in the morning
- Dizziness, blurred vision, or nosebleeds in severe cases
- Shortness of breath and chest discomfort
- Palpitations

**Hypertensive Crisis** (BP above 180/120 mmHg) is a medical emergency: severe headache, confusion, visual disturbances, chest pain — go to hospital immediately.

**Management:**
- Reduce salt intake
- Eat more fruits and vegetables
- Exercise regularly
- Avoid tobacco, excessive alcohol, and khat
- Take prescribed medication daily without stopping — even when feeling well

### Condition: Diabetes Mellitus

**Profile:**
Diabetes is a metabolic disorder characterized by chronic high blood sugar due to insulin resistance (Type 2) or absolute insulin deficiency (Type 1). Over 25 million people in Africa live with diabetes, and the majority are undiagnosed.

**Classic Symptoms (The Three P's):**
- Polyuria: abnormally frequent urination, especially at night
- Polydipsia: intense, unquenchable thirst
- Polyphagia: constant extreme hunger

**Other Symptoms:**
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing wounds and sores
- Recurring skin or vaginal infections
- Tingling or burning in hands and feet (peripheral neuropathy)
- Unexplained weight loss (Type 1)

**When to Seek Care:**
Any person with these symptoms should have a blood glucose test. Uncontrolled diabetes leads to kidney failure, blindness, amputation, and heart disease.

---

### Condition: Cardiovascular Disease and Stroke

**Profile:**
Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) include conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels — coronary artery disease, heart failure, and stroke. They are the leading cause of death globally and a rapidly growing cause of death in Africa.

**Warning Signs of a Heart Attack:**
- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness — may radiate to the left arm, neck, jaw, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Nausea, cold sweats, and dizziness
- Unusual fatigue

**Warning Signs of a Stroke (Use FAST):**
- Face drooping on one side
- Arm weakness — inability to raise both arms equally
- Speech difficulty — slurred or confused speech
- Time to call emergency services immediately

**Risk Reduction:**
Control blood pressure and blood sugar, stop smoking, maintain healthy weight, exercise regularly, and reduce salt and saturated fat intake.

### Condition: Sickle Cell Disease

**Profile:**
Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a hereditary blood disorder most prevalent in Sub-Saharan Africa, where approximately 75% of the global SCD burden occurs. It is caused by a mutation in the hemoglobin gene, causing red blood cells to take on a rigid, sickle shape that blocks blood flow, causes severe pain, and damages organs.

**Symptoms:**
- Episodes of severe pain (vaso-occlusive crises) in the chest, abdomen, joints, and bones
- Anemia: fatigue, pale skin, yellowing of the eyes (jaundice)
- Swollen hands and feet (dactylitis) — often the first sign in infants
- Frequent infections due to reduced spleen function
- Delayed growth in children
- Stroke risk — even in young children

**Management:**
- Daily penicillin prophylaxis in children under 5 to prevent infections
- Folic acid supplementation
- Hydroxyurea to reduce crisis frequency
- Adequate hydration and avoiding extreme temperatures
- Immediate care during any painful crisis, chest pain, or fever

## SECTION 3: EMERGENCY RED FLAGS

The following symptoms require immediate transfer to a health facility — do not wait:

- Fever with confusion, convulsions, or inability to stand
- Difficulty breathing or very fast breathing at rest
- Sudden severe headache with neck stiffness
- Non-blanching purple or red rash with fever
- Profuse watery diarrhea with signs of dehydration in a child
- Jaundice in a newborn baby
- Chest pain radiating to the arm or jaw
- Sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, or speech difficulty
- Heavy vaginal bleeding during or after pregnancy
- A child who cannot be woken up

## SECTION 4: HOME SUPPORT STRATEGIES

These strategies support recovery but do not replace professional diagnosis or treatment:

- **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS):** Mix one ORS sachet in one litre of clean water. Give small sips frequently. ORS is available free at health posts and pharmacies across Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia, and Kenya.
- **Fever management:** Paracetamol at the correct weight-based dose. Tepid sponging. Never give aspirin to children.
- **Hydration:** Clean water, clear broths, and coconut water help maintain fluid balance during illness.
- **Honey for cough (adults and children over 12 months only):** Half to one teaspoon can soothe throat irritation. Never give honey to infants under 12 months — risk of infant botulism.
- **Salt water gargle:** Quarter to half teaspoon of salt in a glass of warm water to reduce throat inflammation.
- **Rest and continued feeding:** During illness, especially in children, continued feeding prevents malnutrition-infection cycles.

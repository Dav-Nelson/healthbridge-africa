# Nigeria Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

## Condition: Malaria

### Profile
Malaria remains an intense public health challenge in Nigeria, accounting for roughly 27% of global malaria cases. It is caused by the *Plasmodium falciparum* parasite, transmitted via the bite of an infected female *Anopheles* mosquito. It is highly preventable and curable if intercepted early.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Sudden onset of high fever and shivering chills.
* Severe headache, generalized joint pain, and muscle aches.
* Profuse sweating, fatigue, and loss of appetite.
* Nausea, vomiting, and mild diarrhea (frequently misdiagnosed as typhoid).

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Malaria, Plasmodium falciparum, Anopheles.
* **Nigerian Pidgin:** "Body dey hot", "heavy cold", "malaria fever", "shivering".
* **Hausa:** Zazzabin cizon sauro.
* **Yoruba:** Iba kulu-kulu, iba rebe-rebe.
* **Igbo:** Akwụkwụ ịba, ịba anwụnta.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Diagnostic Mandate:** Every suspected case must undergo parasitological confirmation via a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or microscopy *before* initiating treatment to prevent drug-resistant strains.
* **First-Line Intervention:** Uncomplicated malaria must be treated using artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT). The approved national regimens are:
  * Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)
  * Artesunate-Amodiaquine (AA)
* **Severe Malaria Protocol:** If a patient exhibits persistent vomiting, severe anemia, dark urine, or convulsions, they must be transitioned immediately to an emergency center for intravenous Artesunate.
* **Prevention Standards:** Use of Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs), Intermittent Preventive Treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) using Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine (SP), and Seasonal Malaria Chemoprevention (SMC) in northern states.

## Condition: Lassa Fever

### Profile
Lassa fever is an acute viral hemorrhagic fever endemic to Nigeria, caused by the Lassa virus. The primary animal reservoir is the multi-mammate rat (*Mastomys natalensis*). Transmission occurs through direct contact with rat urine, feces, or saliva on contaminated food, surfaces, or open wounds.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* **Early Stage:** Gradual onset of fever, general body weakness, severe headache, and sore throat (looks like a standard respiratory infection).
* **Advanced Stage:** Facial swelling, bleeding from the nose, mouth, or gastrointestinal tract, low blood pressure, and sudden deafness.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Lassa fever, Mastomys rat, Hemorrhagic, Ribavirin.
* **Nigerian Pidgin:** "Rat sickness", "bleeding fever", "dirty food sickness".
* **Hausa:** Zazzabin Lassa.
* **Yoruba:** Iba Lassa.
* **Igbo:** Fever Lassa.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Immediate Drug Therapy:** The gold-standard antiviral treatment is **Ribavirin**. It is highly effective only if administered early in the course of clinical onset (within the first 6 days of fever).
* **Infection Prevention and Control (IPC):** Strict isolation of suspected patients. Healthcare workers must wear complete Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) due to high human-to-human transmission risks via bodily fluids.
* **Environmental Controls:** Store all food supplies (garri, rice, beans) in tightly sealed, rodent-proof plastic or glass containers. Avoid spreading foodstuffs on open roads to dry.

## Condition: Cholera and Acute Watery Diarrhea (AWD)

### Profile
Cholera is a highly contagious bacterial infection caused by ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*. It causes severe, hyper-acute watery diarrhea and can lead to fatal dehydration in less than 24 hours if untreated. It peaks heavily during the rainy season in high-density urban areas.

### Symptoms and Clinical Signs
* Sudden, painless, profuse watery diarrhea (characteristically described as "rice-water stools").
* Frequent projectile vomiting.
* Signs of severe dehydration: Sunken eyes, dry mouth, loss of skin elasticity (skin turgor), low blood pressure, and extreme thirst.

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Cholera, Vibrio cholerae, Rice-water stool, AWD.
* **Nigerian Pidgin:** "Running stomach", "vomit and stool", "water-water toilet".
* **Hausa:** Zawo da amai (Diarrhea and vomiting), kwalara.
* **Yoruba:** Onigba-meji, awo-meji (Vomiting and stooling simultaneously).
* **Igbo:** Mmiri ọsịsa na agbọ agbọ.

### National Treatment Guidelines
* **Immediate Rehydration (Critical):** The primary intervention is aggressive fluid replenishment. Do not wait for hospital admission; start **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)** immediately at home.
* **Zinc Supplementation:** Administer Zinc tablets to pediatric patients (10–20 mg daily for 10–14 days) to shorten the duration of diarrhea.
* **Antibiotic Use:** Reserved strictly for severe cases of dehydration to reduce fluid volume output, utilizing Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin under strict clinical oversight.
* **Sanitation Protocol:** Boil or treat municipal water using chlorine agents (e.g., Water-Guard) before drinking. Enforce strict handwashing protocols.

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health (Care Standards)

### Profile
Nigeria accounts for a significant proportion of global maternal deaths. The Federal Ministry of Health prioritizes the elimination of postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), pre-eclampsia, and neonatal sepsis through standardized antenatal tracking and safe institutional delivery.

### High-Risk Signs to Watch
* **Pregnancy:** Severe persistent headache or blurred vision (signs of pre-eclampsia/high blood pressure), swollen legs, or any vaginal bleeding.
* **Postpartum:** Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking more than two sanitary pads in an hour—postpartum hemorrhage).
* **Infant / Neonate:** High fever, continuous crying, inability to breastfeed, yellowing of eyes, or fast breathing (signs of neonatal sepsis).

### Local Dialect and Keyword Cross-References
* **English / Medical:** Antenatal Care (ANC), Postpartum Hemorrhage, Pre-eclampsia, Sepsis.
* **Nigerian Pidgin:** "Belle bleeding", "fit", "baby body dey hot", "strong eye".
* **Hausa:** Jinin haihuwa (postpartum bleeding), hawan jini na masu juna biyu.
* **Yoruba:** Ẹ̀jẹ̀ lẹ́yìn bímọ, jinjin (convulsions/eclampsia).
* **Igbo:** Ọbara ọmụmụ, ọgbụgba mgba n'ime ime.

### National Care Guidelines
* **Antenatal Schedule:** Every pregnant woman is mandated to attend a minimum of **8 Antenatal Care (ANC) visits** at a certified healthcare center to map fetal development and monitor maternal blood pressure.
* **First-Line Hemorrhage Management:** Administration of **Oxytocin** (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately following the delivery of the infant to induce uterine contraction and arrest bleeding.
* **Tetanus Immunization:** Provision of Tetanus Toxoid (TT) injections during ANC checkups to eliminate maternal and neonatal tetanus risk vectors entirely.

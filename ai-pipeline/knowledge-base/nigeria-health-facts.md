# Nigeria Public Health Guidelines & Clinical Interventions

---

## Condition: Malaria

**Profile:**
Malaria is Nigeria's single greatest public health burden, accounting for approximately 27% of global malaria cases. It is caused by Plasmodium falciparum and transmitted by infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Nigeria records an estimated 68 million malaria cases annually, with children under five and pregnant women at highest risk. It is preventable and curable when caught early.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Malaria, malaria fever
- Pidgin: "Body dey hot", "I dey shake", "Heavy cold", "Malaria don catch me", "Shivering"

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever with shivering chills and sweating
- Severe headache, joint pain, and muscle aches
- Profuse sweating, deep fatigue, and loss of appetite
- Nausea, vomiting, and mild diarrhea — frequently misdiagnosed as typhoid
- In children: refusal to feed, lethargy, convulsions, and fast breathing are danger signs

**Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Hospital Care:**
- Convulsions or loss of consciousness
- Inability to stand or sit up
- Repeated vomiting preventing oral medication
- Dark or cola-colored urine (blackwater fever)
- Severe pallor or yellowing of the eyes

**National Treatment Guidelines (NCDC/FMoH):**
- Every suspected case must be confirmed with a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or microscopy before treatment — do not treat based on symptoms alone
- First-line treatment for uncomplicated malaria: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) or Artesunate-Amodiaquine (AA)
- Severe malaria: intravenous Artesunate at a hospital — do not attempt home treatment
- Pregnant women: Intermittent Preventive Treatment using Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine (IPTp-SP) from the second trimester
- Prevention: sleep under Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs) every night; use indoor residual spraying where available

## Condition: Typhoid Fever

**Profile:**
Typhoid fever is endemic across Nigeria, particularly in densely populated urban areas with unreliable water supply and poor sanitation. It is caused by Salmonella Typhi and spreads through food or water contaminated with infected feces. It is frequently confused with malaria — testing both simultaneously is common practice in Nigerian clinics.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Typhoid, typhoid fever, enteric fever
- Pidgin: "Typhoid don catch me", "Serious fever wey no gree go", "Stomach fever"

**Symptoms:**
- Gradually rising fever in a step-wise pattern — unlike malaria's cyclic pattern
- Severe persistent headache and general malaise
- Abdominal pain, bloating, and constipation (common in adults) or diarrhea (common in children)
- Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
- Rose spots — faint salmon-colored rash on the trunk in the second week
- Enlarged liver and spleen
- Confusion and altered mental status in severe untreated cases

**Complications Requiring Emergency Care:**
Intestinal perforation and internal bleeding are life-threatening complications of untreated typhoid. Any patient with severe abdominal pain, rigid abdomen, or sudden deterioration must go to hospital immediately.

**National Treatment Guidelines:**
- Diagnosis: Widal test or blood culture — always confirm before treating
- First-line antibiotics: Azithromycin or Ciprofloxacin (check local resistance patterns)
- Drug-resistant typhoid (MDR) is increasing in Nigeria — Ceftriaxone IV for resistant cases
- Always complete the full antibiotic course — stopping early causes relapse
- Prevention: boil or treat drinking water; wash hands before eating and after using the toilet; avoid street food from unhygienic sources

## Condition: Lassa Fever

**Profile:**
Lassa fever is an acute viral hemorrhagic fever endemic to Nigeria, caused by the Lassa virus. The primary reservoir is the multimammate rat (Mastomys natalensis). Transmission occurs through direct contact with rat urine, feces, or saliva on contaminated food or surfaces. Nigeria records seasonal outbreaks annually, predominantly in Edo, Ondo, Bauchi, and Ebonyi states.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Lassa fever, hemorrhagic fever
- Pidgin: "Rat sickness", "Bleeding fever", "Dirty food sickness"

**Symptoms:**
- Early stage (looks like a common infection): gradual onset of fever, weakness, severe headache, and sore throat
- Advanced stage: facial swelling, bleeding from the nose, mouth, or gastrointestinal tract, low blood pressure, chest and abdominal pain
- Sudden deafness — a distinctive late complication even in recovering patients

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Any fever with bleeding from any body opening — nose, mouth, gums, eyes, or rectum — is a medical emergency. Isolate the patient and go to the nearest hospital.

**National Treatment Guidelines:**
- Gold-standard antiviral treatment: Ribavirin — most effective within the first 6 days of fever onset
- Strict isolation of all suspected cases; healthcare workers must use full PPE
- Prevention: store all food (garri, rice, beans) in tightly sealed rodent-proof containers; never spread food on open ground to dry; block rat entry points in homes

## Condition: Cholera

**Profile:**
Cholera outbreaks occur annually in Nigeria, peaking during the rainy season in high-density urban communities with contaminated water supplies. It is caused by Vibrio cholerae and can cause fatal dehydration within hours if untreated.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Cholera, acute watery diarrhea
- Pidgin: "Running stomach", "Vomit and stool", "Water-water toilet", "E dey purge me die"

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden, painless, profuse watery diarrhea — characteristic "rice-water" appearance
- Frequent projectile vomiting
- Rapid severe dehydration: sunken eyes, dry mouth, loss of skin elasticity, no urination, muscle cramps
- In children: extreme lethargy and cold skin are danger signs

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Any person — especially a child — with profuse diarrhea and vomiting must begin ORS immediately and go to a health facility without delay. Do not wait.

**National Treatment Guidelines (NCDC):**
- Begin Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) immediately at home — do not wait for hospital
- Severe dehydration: intravenous Ringer's Lactate at a health facility
- Children: Zinc supplementation (10–20 mg daily for 10–14 days)
- Antibiotics (Azithromycin) reserved for severe cases only
- Prevention: boil or treat all drinking water; wash hands with soap and water; use Water-Guard or similar water treatment products

## Condition: Tuberculosis (TB)

**Profile:**
Nigeria ranks among the top high-burden TB countries globally. TB is airborne — spread when a person with active pulmonary TB coughs, sneezes, or spits. TB/HIV co-infection significantly increases severity and mortality. Treatment is free at all public health facilities.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: TB, tuberculosis, chest disease
- Pidgin: "Chest cough", "E dey cough blood", "Chest problem wey no dey go"

**Symptoms:**
- Persistent cough lasting more than two weeks — may produce blood-stained sputum
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Night sweats and persistent low-grade fever
- Extreme fatigue and general weakness

**National Treatment Guidelines (NCDC):**
- Diagnosis: GeneXpert molecular test (preferred) or sputum microscopy
- Treatment: 6-month course of Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol — completely free at all public health facilities
- Never stop treatment early — incomplete treatment creates drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)
- All TB patients must be tested for HIV; all HIV patients must be screened for TB
- Contact tracing: all household contacts of a confirmed TB case must be screened

## Condition: Meningitis

**Profile:**
Nigeria sits within Africa's "Meningitis Belt" — northern states including Sokoto, Kebbi, Zamfara, Katsina, Kano, and Jigawa experience epidemic meningococcal meningitis outbreaks, typically during the dry harmattan season (December–June). It is caused by Neisseria meningitidis and is a medical emergency.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Meningitis, brain fever
- Pidgin: "Brain fever", "Neck wey strong", "Head wey dey pain die"

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden severe headache — described as the worst headache of the person's life
- High fever and stiff neck — unable to touch chin to chest
- Sensitivity to light and loud sounds
- Nausea, vomiting, and confusion
- Non-blanching purple or red rash — a medical emergency sign
- In infants: bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, refusal to feed, blank staring

**When to Seek Immediate Care:**
Fever + stiff neck + severe headache = go to hospital immediately. Every hour of delay increases risk of death or permanent disability (deafness, brain damage, limb loss).

**Prevention:**
Meningococcal vaccines (MenAfriVac for serogroup A) are available in northern states during outbreak seasons.

## Condition: Malnutrition

**Profile:**
Malnutrition remains a significant public health challenge in Nigeria, particularly in the northwest and northeast geopolitical zones. Approximately 2.5 million Nigerian children suffer from Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) annually. It is both a cause and consequence of infectious disease.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Malnutrition, kwashiorkor, marasmus, stunting, wasting
- Pidgin: "Pikin wey no get food", "Hungry sickness", "Swollen belle pikin"

**Symptoms:**
- In children: visible muscle wasting, prominent ribs and collarbones
- Mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) below 11.5 cm = severe acute malnutrition
- Swollen face, feet, and legs (kwashiorkor)
- Pale, dry, peeling skin and thin, discolored hair
- Extreme lethargy and irritability
- Persistent infections and slow wound healing

**National Treatment Guidelines (FMoH):**
- Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM): treat with Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF — Plumpy'Nut) at a Stabilisation Centre or Community-based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) site
- Do not give regular food to a severely malnourished child without medical guidance
- Prevention: exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months; appropriate complementary feeding from 6 months; routine Vitamin A supplementation and deworming

## Condition: Maternal and Neonatal Health

**Profile:**
Nigeria accounts for a disproportionate share of global maternal deaths. The leading causes are postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia, sepsis, and obstructed labor. The Federal Ministry of Health mandates skilled attendance at all deliveries and a minimum of 8 antenatal care visits.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Pregnancy complications, postpartum hemorrhage, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia
- Pidgin: "Belle bleeding", "Fit for belle", "Baby body dey hot", "Strong eye for belle"

**Danger Signs During Pregnancy (Seek Care Immediately):**
- Severe persistent headache or blurred vision — signs of pre-eclampsia
- Swollen face, hands, or legs
- Any vaginal bleeding during pregnancy
- Reduced or absent fetal movement
- Fever with lower abdominal pain

**Danger Signs After Delivery:**
- Heavy vaginal bleeding — soaking more than two pads per hour (postpartum hemorrhage)
- High fever with foul-smelling vaginal discharge (infection/sepsis)
- Convulsions (eclampsia)

**Danger Signs in Newborns:**
- High fever or cold to the touch
- Continuous crying or inability to be consoled
- Inability to breastfeed or swallow
- Yellowing of eyes or skin within 24 hours of birth
- Fast or difficult breathing

**National Care Guidelines (FMoH):**
- Minimum 8 Antenatal Care (ANC) visits at a certified health facility
- Oxytocin (10 IU intramuscularly) immediately after delivery to prevent postpartum hemorrhage
- Tetanus Toxoid (TT) injections during ANC
- All deliveries should occur at a health facility with a skilled birth attendant
- Newborn: Vitamin K injection, eye drops, and BCG and Hepatitis B vaccines at birth

## Condition: HIV and AIDS

**Profile:**
Nigeria has the second largest HIV epidemic in the world, with approximately 1.9 million people living with HIV. Despite significant progress, treatment coverage remains below target. HIV/TB co-infection is a major driver of TB deaths in Nigeria.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: HIV, AIDS, immune deficiency
- Pidgin: "AIDS", "HIV sickness", "The sickness wey no get cure"

**Symptoms:**
- Early: flu-like illness 2–4 weeks after exposure — fever, rash, swollen lymph nodes, sore throat
- Latent stage: asymptomatic for years
- Advanced (AIDS): rapid weight loss, chronic diarrhea, oral thrush, recurring infections, night sweats, severe fatigue

**National Treatment Guidelines (NCDC):**
- HIV testing is free and confidential at all public health facilities
- Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) is free and must be started as soon as possible after diagnosis
- Preferred first-line regimen: Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir (TLD)
- All HIV-positive patients must be screened for TB at every visit
- Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission (PMTCT): all pregnant women must be tested; HIV-positive mothers receive ART throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding

## Condition: Dengue Fever

**Profile:**
Dengue fever is an emerging public health concern in Nigeria, with confirmed outbreaks documented in Lagos, Abeokuta, and other urban centers. It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, which bites during the day — unlike the Anopheles mosquito responsible for malaria.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Dengue, dengue fever, breakbone fever
- Pidgin: "Another kind malaria", "Bone pain fever", "Day mosquito fever"

**Symptoms:**
- Sudden high fever (39–40°C) lasting 2–7 days
- Severe headache and pain behind the eyes
- Intense muscle and joint pain — "breakbone fever"
- Nausea, vomiting, and swollen glands
- Rash appearing 3–4 days into the fever
- Severe dengue: bleeding from gums or nose, blood in vomit or stool — medical emergency

**Treatment:**
- No specific antiviral — supportive care: rest, fluids, and paracetamol for fever
- Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen — they increase bleeding risk
- Severe dengue requires hospital admission for IV fluids and monitoring

## Condition: Sickle Cell Disease

**Profile:**
Nigeria has the highest burden of sickle cell disease in the world, with approximately 150,000 babies born with sickle cell anaemia each year — the highest globally. One in four Nigerians carries the sickle cell trait. It is a hereditary condition, not infectious.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Sickle cell, sickle cell anaemia, SS disease
- Pidgin: "SS blood", "Sickle cell", "Blood wey bend"

**Symptoms:**
- Episodes of severe pain (crises) in the chest, abdomen, joints, and bones
- Chronic anemia: fatigue, pallor, yellowish eyes
- Swollen hands and feet — often the first sign in infants
- Frequent infections due to reduced spleen function
- Delayed growth and development in children
- Stroke risk — even in young children and infants

**Management:**
- Daily penicillin prophylaxis for children under 5
- Daily folic acid supplementation
- Hydroxyurea to reduce crisis frequency — available at tertiary hospitals
- Adequate hydration at all times
- Immediate care for any painful crisis, chest pain, fever, or signs of stroke
- Newborn screening is recommended — know your genotype before marriage

## Condition: Monkeypox (Mpox)

**Profile:**
Nigeria reported the world's largest mpox outbreak outside Central Africa in 2017–2018 and has recorded ongoing cases since. It spreads through close physical contact with an infected person's rash, bodily fluids, or respiratory droplets during prolonged face-to-face contact.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Monkeypox, mpox
- Pidgin: "New rash sickness", "Monkey sickness"

**Symptoms:**
- Fever, severe headache, swollen lymph nodes, back pain, and muscle aches — 5–21 days after exposure
- Rash developing 1–3 days after fever — begins on the face, spreads to palms, soles, and body
- Rash progresses: flat spots → raised bumps → blisters → pustules → scabs
- Lesions are deep-seated, well-defined, and may be painful

**When to Seek Care:**
Unexplained rash with fever and swollen lymph nodes should be evaluated. Avoid close contact with others. Report to the NCDC.

## Condition: Scabies

**Profile:**
Scabies is common in overcrowded households and schools across Nigeria. It spreads through prolonged skin-to-skin contact and shared bedding or clothing.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Scabies, itch mite
- Pidgin: "Body dey scratch me die", "Itch wey no gree stop", "Mite scratch"

**Symptoms:**
- Intense itching, especially at night
- Pimple-like rash with small red bumps and blisters
- Thin burrow tracks between fingers, on wrists, elbows, armpits, waistline, and genitals
- In infants: rash may also appear on head, face, palms, and soles

**Treatment:**
- Permethrin 5% cream: apply to the entire body from neck to toes, leave for 8–12 hours, wash off
- All household members and close contacts must be treated simultaneously
- Wash all bedding and clothing in hot water on the same day as treatment
- Repeat treatment after one week

## Condition: Ringworm (Tinea)

**Profile:**
Ringworm is a fungal skin infection very common in Nigerian children and adults. Despite the name, it is not caused by a worm. It thrives in warm, humid conditions and spreads through direct contact.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Ringworm, fungal infection
- Pidgin: "Round round rash", "Fungus wey dey spread", "Ring for body"

**Symptoms:**
- Circular, red, scaly ring-shaped patches with a clear center and raised edges (body)
- Scaly patches on the scalp with hair loss (common in children)
- Scaling, itching, and cracking between the toes (athlete's foot)
- Red, itchy rash in the groin area (jock itch)

**Treatment:**
- Body and foot: topical antifungal cream (clotrimazole or miconazole) twice daily for 2–4 weeks
- Scalp ringworm: oral antifungal medication (griseofulvin) — topical creams do not work on the scalp
- Keep affected areas clean and dry; do not share combs, towels, or hats

## Condition: Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

**Profile:**
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It is not contagious. On darker skin — as is typical in Nigeria — eczema may appear as dark brown, purple, or grey patches rather than the red appearance described in many medical textbooks. This frequently leads to misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Eczema, atopic dermatitis, skin rash
- Pidgin: "Skin wey dey dry and scratch", "Rash wey no dey go", "Dry skin sickness"

**Symptoms:**
- Dry, sensitive skin with intense itching — especially at night
- Dark brown, grey, or purple patches on darker skin
- Rough, leathery, or scaly patches
- Common locations: inside of elbows, behind knees, hands, feet, face, and neck
- Skin may weep or crust if scratched and infected

**Management:**
- Moisturize daily with fragrance-free emollients (shea butter is effective and widely available in Nigeria)
- Use mild, unscented soap; avoid hot water baths
- Hydrocortisone cream (1%) for flare-ups
- Avoid triggers: harsh soaps, dust, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Seek care if skin becomes warm, weeping, or crusted — signs of infection

## Condition: Conjunctivitis (Apollo)

**Profile:**
Conjunctivitis — known widely in Nigeria as "Apollo" — is inflammation of the eye's outer membrane. Infectious conjunctivitis is highly contagious and spreads through direct contact with eye secretions, contaminated hands, or shared items. Outbreaks occur frequently in schools and households.

**How Nigerians Describe It:**
- English: Conjunctivitis, pink eye
- Pidgin: "Apollo", "Red eye", "Eye wey dey pain and red"

**Symptoms:**
- Red or pink coloring in the white of the eye
- Thick yellow or green discharge crusting the eyelashes — especially on waking
- Increased tearing and burning sensation
- Feeling of sand or grit in the eye
- Sensitivity to light

**Treatment:**
- Bacterial: antibiotic eye drops or ointment (chloramphenicol)
- Viral: no antibiotic needed — resolves in 1–2 weeks
- Clean discharge gently with a clean, damp cloth
- Do not share towels, pillows, or eye drops
- Wash hands frequently; keep children home from school until discharge clears
